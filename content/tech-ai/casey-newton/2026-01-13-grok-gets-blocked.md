On Friday, following days of mounting outrage over Grok generating sexualized deepfakes of women and children, X said it would restrict the feature to paying subscribers. For a brief moment, it appeared as if Elon Musk‚Äôs xAI had begun to address the harms his platform is enabling: generating thousands of sexualized images on demand every hour, and hosting them publicly for anyone to view.

But as Robert Hart at The Verge quickly discovered, the ‚Äúrestriction‚Äù applied only to one method of accessing the bot [https://www.platformer.news/r/c2d7b3fa?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] ‚Äî tagging @grok in a reply to an X post. The "edit image" button still appears on every image on X's desktop site. Long-pressing any image in the mobile app still invokes Grok. The standalone Grok website and the Grok tab throughout X remain fully functional. Hart tested all of these with free accounts. And Grok readily complied with requests to "nudify" images.

Victims include Ashley St. Clair, a conservative influencer and a mother of one of Musk‚Äôs children, who found X users creating sexualized images of her [https://www.platformer.news/r/031dfda2?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] based on a photo of her taken when she was 14. They also include the Princess of Wales [https://www.platformer.news/r/08ddd82d?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670], the host of Love Island, and countless less prominent women and children. People who had posted innocuous photos of gym workouts or even author photos found that accounts were invading their mentions and asking Grok to enlarge their breasts or cover their faces in ‚Äúdonut glaze.‚Äù

It is bad enough that the safeguards in Grok‚Äôs underlying model did not stop these images from being generated. That they were attached to the accounts of the victims, most of whom were alerted to each fresh violation via a push notification, represents a new low in the history of content moderation for a major platform.

In response, Musk has vacillated between his usual üòÇ routine and bitter defiance. On Jan. 2, when an X user posted that "Grok's viral image moment has arrived, it's a little different than the Ghibli one," Musk responded [https://www.platformer.news/r/ee2c0f80?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670]: "Way funnier üòÇ." The day before, Musk had playfully requested [https://www.platformer.news/r/8a5211b1?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] Grok put himself in a bikini, responding "Perfect" to the result. The following day, he posted that users making "illegal content will suffer the same consequences as if they upload illegal content" ‚Äî placing blame on users rather than the tool he had given them.

On Friday, after regulators had begun to threaten action against X, Musk posted [https://www.platformer.news/r/2f714c63?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] that critics "want any excuse for censorship" and shared an AI-generated image of UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer in a bikini with the caption "They just want to suppress free speech."

This schtick still plays with US regulators and Republican lawmakers, some of whom issued murmurs of concern [https://www.platformer.news/r/4e014c0f?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] but stopped short of taking more substantive action. And the threat of backlash from the Trump Administration has been enough to cow Apple and Google into sustained inaction, remaining silent for more than a week as an app hosted on their stores has been used to generate non-consensual sexual imagery, including content that regulators say may be illegal (potentially including CSAM).

As of Monday, though, the dam is beginning to break ‚Äî in a few countries, at least.

Here‚Äôs Laura Cress at the BBC [https://www.platformer.news/r/4c0d2ceb?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670]:

The UK will bring into force a law which will make it illegal to create non-consensual intimate images, following widespread concerns over Elon Musk's Grok AI chatbot.

Technology Secretary Liz Kendall said the government would also seek to make it illegal for companies to supply the tools designed to create such images.

Speaking to the Commons, Kendall said AI-generated pictures of women and children in states of undress, created without a person's consent, were not "harmless images" but "weapons of abuse".

Separately, UK regulator Ofcom said Monday that it had launched an investigation [https://www.platformer.news/r/d320347b?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] into the scandal. If found culpable, Ofcom can issue a fine of up to 10 percent of X‚Äôs global revenue.

The UK‚Äôs move followed an outright ban on Grok in both Indonesia and Malaysia [https://www.platformer.news/r/fcf56950?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] ‚Äî the first time an AI chatbot had been banned by a nation-state in response to the content of the material it generated. Notably, both countries have strict laws against pornography ‚Äî even of the consensual, human-created variety.

France [https://www.platformer.news/r/f21ae100?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670], Australia [https://www.platformer.news/r/a8932a35?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670], and India [https://www.platformer.news/r/c53223c6?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] have also all announced investigations into Grok. In truth, though, there is very little to investigate. Thousands of public posts tell the grim story. No one is disputing that xAI made available to its user base a tool that puts women and children into their underwear ‚Äî or worse ‚Äî against their will. No one is disputing that some of this content is illegal in many of the countries where xAI operates. And no one is disputing that Grok continues to generate this kind of material [https://www.platformer.news/r/48f9d189?m=5fdc9f67-a5ab-42ee-ab4f-91ba7b47c670] on demand.

When deepfakes first emerged as an online harm, I took some comfort in the notion that they likely would not spread virally. Individuals might send them via messages, I thought, but platforms would intervene to remove violating posts ‚Äî particularly posts targeting children. Never could I have imagined that someday the former Twitter would deploy tools that enable and amplify those harms, and would dismiss any attempts to rein the company in as ‚Äúcensorship.‚Äù

The question now, then, is not about what happened. We know what happened. Rather, it‚Äôs about which lawmakers and regulators will have the courage to stand up against Musk and X and defend the principle that online abuse is real abuse ‚Äî and that people should be protected from it.
