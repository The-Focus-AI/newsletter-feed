Of Montréal

by Eli Zeger

Earlier this month, I went to grab dinner and a drink at a hip bar in Montréal’s Mile End neighborhood. The weather was finally getting nicer and I had just gotten paid for a copywriting gig. Plus I liked the outfit I had on that day. My plan was to eat and walk around for a while before heading back to my apartment. But when I got my check, the server asked me, “Are you here with the movie?”

What movie? “Mile End Kicks. It’s about what Montréal was like in 2011. A bunch of people from the cast were here because the premiere’s tonight.” I knew nothing about it.

Walking back down Avenue Bernard, I saw a line outside Théâtre Outremont of at least a hundred people. The poster for Mile End Kicks was displayed on the digital marquee. I asked someone at the end of the line if they were all waiting to buy tickets, and he told me the premiere was invite-only. How exclusive! Whenever I stumble upon an event like this, I always have the irrational thought: How come I wasn’t invited? After most of the line had made its way into the theater, I approached the doorman and (somewhat) lied to him that I was with the press; I said that I had gotten approval at the last minute, so my name might not be on the list.

A few clusters of people had been waiting to see if they could get in. The doorman saw that I was a party of one and handed me a ticket.

----------------------------------------

 

I moved to Montréal for grad school in August 2024. I wanted to be outside of the U.S. but not too far away, and I sought cheaper rent than what I had been paying in NYC. Another draw was Montréal’s history of radical music and politics. The city is home to Godspeed You! Black Emperor and the second-largest housing co-op [https://flaminghydra.com/r/b83e7bca?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] in North America. At the end of each summer, an experimental electronic music festival [https://flaminghydra.com/r/d7b91e3c?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] takes over downtown. On every other block there’s some kind of coopérative de solidarité—a business that is collectively owned by both its workers and customers.

I knew that my preconceived image of Montréal was bound to differ wildly from the real thing. Inflation and gentrification are happening everywhere, and, as in all major cities, there are fewer venues, galleries, and cultural spaces than there were before Covid. But some of this city’s drawbacks are unique.

The right-wing Coalition Avenir Québec (CAQ), which controls Québec’s provincial government, has been ramping up its Francophilic revanchism over the last few years. Bill 96, passed in 2022 [https://flaminghydra.com/r/2f152dd5?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], makes it harder to find work or access government services if you aren’t fluent in French. The legislation is titled “An Act respecting French, the official and common language of Québec [https://flaminghydra.com/r/766bee61?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122].”

Then there’s the matter of housing. Bill 31, passed in 2024, effectively put an end to lease transfers, a longstanding legal practice that allowed departing tenants to pass their leases on to new tenants, which kept rents low in the province for decades. Lease transfers made the most sense in a place as populous as Montréal, and so the new law’s impact here is most acute. Newcomers—Anglos and Francos alike—now have fewer and fewer opportunities to find affordable rent.

via guacha.fit, via Facebook

But there is much to love here. Montréal pedestrianizes its busiest streets in the summer, and there are so many great cheap eats in the city, especially around Little Italy and Rosemont: Lalime and Nouveau Système and the weekday breakfast special at Le Vieux Velo (two eggs over easy with bacon, potatoes, and toast for $10 CAD, so like $7 USD). All these are on the way to Parquette, where I go to do computer work and take a dancey exercise class [https://flaminghydra.com/r/8c5f20da?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. And regarding Québec overall, five hours north of Montréal is Saguenay, the hometown of Angine de Poitrine, the first buzzband in years to deserve the attention they are getting. They sound like the missing link between Thee Oh Sees and Horse Lords.

----------------------------------------

 

Mile End Kicks (image via IMDb)

The story of Mile End Kicks is based loosely on the life of the film’s writer-director, Chandler Levack, who lived in Montréal during her early twenties. In the film, Grace, played by Barbie Ferreira, is a music critic eager to ditch Toronto and the stale alt-weekly where she permalances. She finds a sublet in Mile End and lands a book deal with Bloomsbury; her goal is to spend the summer drafting a 33 1/3 book [https://flaminghydra.com/r/4c7b5d08?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] about Alanis Morissette’s Jagged Little Pill. During the screening I saw, everyone in the theater laughed when the publisher offers her an advance of $500 (USD). But hey, at least it covers her rent [https://flaminghydra.com/r/6ada88a4?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] for a month! Actually, it doesn’t. Grace runs out of money, fails to complete her manuscript, and is ordered to return her advance.

Quite a lot of Québecers are having a meltdown over Mile End Kicks. Because the story is a realistic account of an outsider’s experience in the province, part of it is, naturally, about her difficulty finding a job. The film spends less than two minutes acknowledging her limited employment opportunities as a non-French-speaker, but this, it turns out, is too much for the Québecers on r/montreal, many of whom can’t stand how “Anglo” the film is and how ignorant it is of the “local culture.” What would they have preferred, that all the characters went off into the woods to eat maple taffy [https://flaminghydra.com/r/556ac2ac?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] and listen to French Weezer [https://flaminghydra.com/r/f7e6f91b?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]?

I found Levack’s filmmaking style a bit hammy. But I liked the way she depicted the stakes of music criticism at the turn of the 2010s. Grace leaves her Almost Famous poster at her parents’ house, keeping that bygone era of rock journalism in the past. She befriends a slacker rock band and vows to get them press coverage. They could care less that her byline is in a washed-up rag like Spin—they want to know whether she writes for Pitchfork. She volunteers to be their press agent and gets the attention of a critic at the Montréal Mirror, whose coverage generates buzz. (Later, after losing her romantic interest in the slacker band’s douchey frontman, Grace appears to realize that his music sucked all along.) It’s obvious that none of her work pays well, or at all, but it still has some capacity to make or break indie artists.

Grace arrives in Mile End in the summer of 2011, the same year that Pitchfork moved from Chicago to Brooklyn. (Go East, young hipster!) The move commemorated the website’s then-monopoly over tastemaking in the digital age, along with its evolution into a proper media company with a formalized house voice. No more monkey piss [https://flaminghydra.com/r/0f2506f9?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. Their Condé Nast acquisition was still a few years away, but in hindsight it’s clear that Pitchfork was getting ready for it, or for something similar. Legacy music mags had begun publishing more online content to keep pace, investing less and less in print, or abandoning the medium altogether.

Did the collective “pivot to digital” make a difference? 2011 was also the year that Spotify launched in the United States, expanding to Canada in 2014. These were the twilight years for music criticism as music discovery.

Mile End Kicks takes place in the midst of another pivotal moment that isn’t shown or even glanced at in the film. This was the start of the Québec student strikes, otherwise known as the Maple Spring [https://flaminghydra.com/r/2db14ed6?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. In March 2011, the provincial government announced its intent to increase tuition rates at all universities. Student groups responded with some of the most aggressive mobilizations in the history of the Canadian left. Is Levack’s omission of the Maple Spring a glaring oversight? Sure, but in a way it keeps her nostalgia in check. The focus of the movie is on how much quieter, lonelier, and less exciting the city feels than Grace had hoped it would.

Although the Maple Spring reached its peak [https://flaminghydra.com/r/7b24f549?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] in 2012, protests were happening throughout 2011, with one student union after another voting to strike. In Montréal, Anglophone and Francophone millennials were united around this cause. They were all getting screwed over by the same austerity tactics. The culture warring was as frivolous then as it is now.
