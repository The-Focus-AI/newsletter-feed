# A Season in Hell: April 24-

**From:** Lev Grossman <levgrossman@substack.com>
**Date:** Sun, 26 Apr 2026 03:43:43 +0000

A Season in Hell is a diary I kept in 2022-2023 while I was finishing my novel The Bright Sword . When I started this Substack I was sure it would be by far the most popular bit, but it has actually been measurably less popular than the other bits. I thought people would find its raw unvarnished look at the creative process compelling but I guess they also found it a trifle whiny. 
So for the sake of closure, I’m finishing it up. Here are the last few entries. If you’re interested, you can read the rest starting here. 
April 24, 2023
The new draft has gone in to the publisher. I cleaned it up, wiped its nose, stripped out 10,000 words of notes, dumped it out of Scrivener into Microsoft Word, and emailed it to my editor and agent with an awesomely devil-may-care note. Here’s the novel. Sorry it’s a bit late. Hope you like it. 
May 3
Guess who’s back! It’s my novel. The editors like it well enough but it’s too long. There are pacing issues. Also, relatedly, money issues. Apparently the price of paper has skyrocketed since the last time I published a book—because global wood pulp supplies are being eaten up to make all the cardboard boxes people use to ship Amazon purchases—and printing long books is more expensive than it used to be. The profit margins are correspondingly slimmer. So could I maybe make it a little shorter please.
On the one hand I feel resentful that such petty real-world concerns should eat away at my book. Art should exist, if not on an abstract plane, then at least on a plane slightly above the plane of wood-pulp prices. But on the other hand the editors are right. It is too long. In its current form The Bright Sword is almost 225,000 words. The Magicians books are only 145,000 words each. Mrs. Dalloway is only 63,000, for fuck’s sake.
What to do. My editor suggests lifting out (i.e. cutting) three chapters in the middle. But I can’t! My numb fingers refuse to grip the scalpel. What about all the stuff that happens in them? It would be a continuity nightmare! Also I like them. 
I see this more as a crafty nips-and-tucks job. Go after the connective tissue. Quit describing trees all the time. Start scenes later, end’em earlier. And maybe lose the chapter where the knights run around chasing a bird.
May 17
I’m in Auckland, NZ, at the Auckland Writers Festival, when the end comes. 
It’s a great event. Colson Whitehead is here. Eleanor Catton is here. So is Gabrielle Zevin, but I don’t introduce myself because I’m embarrassed that I haven’t read Tomorrow and Tomorrow and Tomorrow.
But I can’t be festive all day long, so I spend a lot of time in my hotel room rereading my book. It is now 35,000 words lighter. And it’s weird because every other time I’ve read it I was constantly having to stop because I stumbled on something that needed fixing. Like when you’re vacuuming and you keep feeling things go crackling and rattling up into the vacuum, and you think, yes, there is more work to be done here. There is more crap on my floor.
But this time I wasn’t stumbling. There wasn’t any crap left on my floor. For the first time in nine years of writing I was just reading my book, and it was almost like reading a book by a writer I actually liked. After nine years of being a Wright brother and watching my kludgy ungainly airship bump along the cold sandy dunes it had finally lifted off and attained powered flight. It was on the wing.
I cried when I got to the end. It’s stupid sitting there crying over your own book, but if I’m not moved by my book, who the hell is going to be moved by it? I still don’t know why I couldn’t simply have written it right the first time, and skipped all this pointless wandering that has consumed approximately a sixth of my life. I think that’s actually partly what the crying was about.
And there’s a tiny bit of sadness too because I know now that The Bright Sword is as good as it’s going to get. It will never get any better. I could have written a Pulitzer-winning book, or a ten-million selling book, or Mrs. Dalloway, but chances are I haven’t. All I’ve written is this one. It’s the best I can do, and that’s all it will ever be. 
But that’s the adventure. The Grail Knight has at last come to Corbenic, where dwells the Fisher King in torment. He has asked the right question. The king is healed. The Waste Land lives. The quest is achieved.
