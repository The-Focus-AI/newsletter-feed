A Hotter World Will Be Pricier

In the summer of 2022, a heatwave in Europe drove temperatures to a 
record-breaking 46C (115F) in Spain, scorching its olive-growing regions. In 
the UK, chickens wilted in the heat, leading to a 9% drop in chicken-meat 
production from the year before. Northern Italy endured its worst drought in 70 
years, slashing harvests of risotto rice.

Researchers calculated that climate change increased temperatures that summer 
in Europe by 1.25C on average — and by as much as 5.7C in some places. This was 
a rare instance in which studies were able to estimate the direct effects of 
climate change on prices, a phenomenon known as “climateflation” that’s only 
beginning to be understood.

Of course, droughts and storms have long affected prices. But as extreme 
weather becomes more common, price spikes once written off as temporary may be 
turning into something more lasting — a powerful new force that could further 
disrupt household and national budgets already beset by an affordability 
crisis. A cadre of economists, climate scientists and central bankers are 
studying climate inflation’s effects, trying to piece together how it moves 
through the economy.

“If you pick up a textbook and you look up the bit on inflation, inflation is 
too much money chasing too few goods. There’s no textbook that says, ‘the 
climate’s cooking and we’re running out of stuff,’” says Mark Blyth, who runs 
Brown University’s Rhodes Center for International Economics and Finance.

While climate shocks mainly push prices up in the short term, they can become 
longer lasting if they change consumer inflation expectations — as research has 
suggested they might.

“The best way to avoid the damages from climate change is to have a global 
green transition,” says Marcus Mølbak Ingholt, senior lead climate economist at 
Danmarks Nationalbank, the Danish central bank. Absent that, a hotter world 
will also likely be a pricier one.

— Emma Court, Bloomberg Green

Executives Who Use AI a Lot Expect More Job Losses

We’ve noted that the people who use AI more often are more excited about it 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-19/how-does-gen-z-feel-about-ai?cmpid=BBD042626_SUNNL&utm_campaign=sundaynl&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260426&utm_content=3817>
 andless worried that it will take their job 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-16/the-bbc-is-at-a-crossroads-after-trump-controversy?cmpid=BBD042626_SUNNL&utm_campaign=sundaynl&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260426&utm_content=3817>
. On the other hand, they seem to be more likely to think that it’ll take your 
job.

Executives who spend more time using AI expect it to cause larger improvements 
in productivity and declines in employment at their firms compared to those 
using it less frequently, according to a survey of 6,000 managers in the US, 
the UK, Germany and Australia.

These relationships are still statistically significant when controlling for 
the age and sector of the executives, according to the researchers, who broke 
out the data in response to questions from Bloomberg Weekend.

“It’s pretty natural,” said Nicholas Bloom, an economist at Stanford and one 
of the study’s authors <https://www.nber.org/papers/w34836>. “Folks using AI 
are predicting large effects, and folks using AI are most likely to be 
protected from it as [they] are riding the tech wave.”

— Walter Frick, Bloomberg Weekend

Scenarios

The year is 2050. “Productivity is high, energy is cheap and plentiful, and 
the economy is thriving,” write the authors ofa new scenario analysis from BCG 
<https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/beyond-tomorrow-four-scenarios-for-the-world-of-2050>
. Governments have come up with rules for the safe use of AI, and most people 
work four days a week. Sounds nice.

Unfortunately, that “AI abundance” scenario is just one of four outlined in 
the BCG report. Two others focus more on geopolitics and climate change than 
AI, but the fourth they dub “digital Darwinism.” In that scenario, AI sparks 
economic growth and even scientific progress — but it “accrue[s] to the tech 
elites in compute-rich nations — and to their investors. Everyone else lives 
more precarious lives.” Gig work proliferates, as does workplace surveillance. 
“Persuasion-focused AI” disrupts political campaigns. “For relief, many escape 
into AI-generated virtual worlds tailored to personal preferences and moods.” (
Maybe the metaverse is still coming 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-23/2026-will-bring-more-ai-and-more-geopolitical-fragmentation?cmpid=BBD042626_SUNNL&utm_campaign=sundaynl&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260426&utm_content=3817>
.)

The difference between these two possible futures is government, says Alan 
Iny, one of the report’s authors and a partner at BCG. “Imagine a world where 
the governments have less power and the corporations have more,” he says of the 
digital Darwinism scenario. “If corporations do come up with some cool cancer 
treatment there is no reason to assume, at least to me, that it will flow 
through to the masses.” In other words,when technical progress outpaces 
politics, bad stuff tends to happen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-12-29/2024-was-the-year-of-bots-bullets-and-bitcoin?cmpid=BBD042626_SUNNL&utm_campaign=sundaynl&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260426&utm_content=3817>
.

The path to the abundance scenario isn’t seamless either. The report imagines 
massive cyberattacks in the 2030s that lead to an AI treaty; it takes a 
disaster to put the world on a better path. “If I just put the AI abundance 
scenario out there people would just say you’re crazy, you’re being naive, 
that’s never going to happen,” says Iny.

— Walter Frick, Bloomberg Weekend

Will Robots Ever Be Able to Make Coffee?

In 2007, Apple co-founder Steve Wozniak predicted that robots would “never” be 
able to make a cup of coffee — meaning walk up to and operate an unfamiliar 
coffee machine. But AI experts now predict that a robot will pass the “coffee 
test” somewhere in the 2030s, with a median prediction of 2034.That’s from the 
LEAP forecasting competition 
<https://forecastingresearch.substack.com/p/when-will-robots-pass-the-coffee> 
run by the Forecasting Research Institute, which we’vewritten about before 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-12/ai-forecasts-don-t-have-to-be-pure-guesswork?cmpid=BBD042626_SUNNL&utm_campaign=sundaynl&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260426&utm_content=3817>
. The “coffee test” here “requires a robot to make a cup of coffee in three 
different unstructured, unfamiliar environments of equivalent complexity to an 
ordinary home, without making any mistakes.”

In addition to forecasts from AI experts, the LEAP project includes 
predictions from so-called “superforecasters” who’ve distinguished themselves 
with accurate predictions in previous tournaments on unrelated topics. And 
their forecasts are slightly more bullish: The median superforecaster predicts 
a robot will pass the coffee test in 2032.

I guess put your coffee machine on notice? Starting next decade it’ll be robot 
pour-overs only.

— Walter Frick, Bloomberg Weekend
