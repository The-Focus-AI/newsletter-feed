IT'S THE WEEKEND

Salmon exposed to cocaine swim farther and behave differently than unexposed fish [https://www.404media.co/r/f30e50f9?m=d9c8ed7d-8f51-45be-865b-ff329f323410], according to the first study to observe the effects of cocaine on fish in the wild rather than a laboratory setting. Many waterways around the world are contaminated with a host of legal and illegal substances that are consumed by humans and then excreted into sewage systems. As global demand for cocaine skyrockets, traces of the drug—including its main metabolite, benzoylecgonine—are flowing into lakes and rivers where they can be absorbed by wildlife, such as Atlantic salmon.

MALUSHIOUS CODE

For a small price, Malus.sh will use AI to ingest any piece of software you give it [https://www.404media.co/r/831212c3?m=d9c8ed7d-8f51-45be-865b-ff329f323410] and spit out a new version of it that “liberates” it from any existing copyright licenses. The result is a new piece of software that serves the same function, but doesn’t have to honor, for example, the kind of copyright licenses that ensure open source software remains free to use and modify, a process which could upend the already fragile open source ecosystem. The site is an elaborate bit of satire designed to bring attention to a very real problem in open source, but it also does exactly what it advertises and is a real LLC that is making money by using AI to produce “clean room” clones of existing software.

EMANUEL: This week we experimented with the podcast format a little bit. We had just finished recording our regular podcast and were just chatting about the fact that Tim Cook stepped down as CEO of Apple. This is exactly the kind of corporate tech news that we don’t normally cover, but we’ve all covered Apple from various angles for the entirety of tenure. We started talking about what Cook’s influence and legacy was and Jason just pressed record and uploaded the conversation. 

People who watched that video seemed to enjoy it. I definitely enjoyed a more casual podcast format so maybe we’ll do more in the future if we can. Please sound off in the comments if it’s something you’re interested in or if there are specific topics/formats you’d like to see in podcast form. 

I wanted to add one more thought about Cook’s legacy that only occurred to me after our recording. As we’ve said, Apple grew spectacularly under Cook’s leadership, and there are various products and services that make up that revenue, but much of it is funneled through Apple stores. That seems obvious on the face of it but it’s pretty odd if you think about it. No other tech company has such a big and iconic presence in brick and mortar retail. Think about all the tech in your house. If it’s not Apple, most likely you got it online or a Best Buy or something like that. Apple stores were a thing before Cook, but there are way more of them, and they’re not just big compared to other tech companies. They are the most profitable retail spaces per square foot [https://www.404media.co/r/7604a909?m=d9c8ed7d-8f51-45be-865b-ff329f323410] that exist. 

Readers familiar with 404 Media lore know that Sam and I are not Apple people, but I have had an iPhone since 2010 or so, so I have been to Apple stores to buy them or ask for repairs. Honestly, I understand the appeal. The stores are pretty, the employees are nice, and it is fun to mess around with the current line of products. I’m always shocked that there’s some kind of workshop happening there whenever I go to an Apple store, and while I’ll never attend one, I respect it and think it’s a good business strategy. 

At least that’s how it used to be. When I was getting ready to go to Sam’s wedding I realized that my Dell laptop is ancient and thought it might be time to try a Macbook Air. My wife had one that I liked years ago and Windows laptops are always kind of a mess in my opinion so I thought I’ll just go to the Apple store and buy one while I’m out running some other errands. It turns out that it’s not that easy?

I talked about this with Joe after the fact, and he agreed that it seems like Apple Stores are not as nice as they used to be. They’re very busy, and at least in the store that I was in, only one designated employee was able to talk to me about and sell me a laptop, and she was busy with someone else. I waited around for 15 minutes before I had to split and ended up not buying a laptop for the trip. Apple Stores also don’t have registers or clear “point of sales,” which is all fine and good if there’s someone available to talk and sell you what you want to buy. If there isn’t someone available, it feels like you’re at a party awkwardly standing in the corner waiting for the opportunity to interject yourself into a conversation. Not the best experience, and next time I need to buy an Apple product I’ll probably just do it online. 

I wouldn’t call it enshittified, but I do think Apple stores falling off are indicative of another part of Cook’s legacy. He made Apple much, much more popular under his leadership, but I think part of that trade off is that overall it feels less like a luxury brand.

JASON: This week I checked in on AI-powered job losses and on the rather dystopia idea that companies are spending money on AI rather than hiring employees at all. Well, Thursday something even bleaker happened. Meta is reallocating money from some 8,000 workers’ salaries (and from closing 6,000 additional open roles) and is basically using that money to make AI chips and construct AI data centers. 

In this case, human beings aren’t exactly being replaced by AI, they are being cast aside so that their salaries can be spent on the physical infrastructure that will then be used to justify further layoffs in the name of efficiency and progress. The remaining employees, meanwhile, will have their mouse movements, keystrokes, and screen contents continuously captured to train AI. 

Here is what Meta told employees in its layoff note: “We're doing this as part of our continued effort to run the company more efficiently and to allow us to offset the other investments we're making.”

The words “offset the other investments we’re making” is doing a ton of heavy lifting here, because the main thing that Meta is “investing” in right now is AI compute, AI chips, and AI data centers. Meta and Mark Zuckerberg have been very public about the fact that it is investing $600 billion in AI data centers over the next two years. Its most recent earnings call was full of Meta executives talking about and justifying their investments in AI infrastructure, “silicon,” and compute capabilities. 

“As we plan for the future, we will continue to invest very significantly in infrastructure to train leading models and deliver personal superintelligence to billions of people and businesses around the world,” Zuckerberg said during the most recent earnings call. “An important part of Meta Compute will be making long term investments in silicon and energy. We will continue working with key partners while advancing our own silicon program.”

“We’re really taking advantage of the current business strength to reinvest a lot of the revenue into what we see as very attractive investment opportunities in AI infrastructure and talent,” Meta CFO Susan Li said on the most recent earnings call. “we are making very significant investments in infrastructure capacity this year to support our AI efforts.” 

What we have then, is a company that is saying it would rather spend money on silicon that it thinks will eventually replace people than on human workers. Somehow I find that to be one of the bleaker AI stories so far.

To change the subject: I’ve been documenting and thinking about and crashing out over the way AI companion apps advertise nonconsent as a perk of their products for a long time. These apps say things like “AI girls never say no” [https://www.404media.co/r/838bef52?m=d9c8ed7d-8f51-45be-865b-ff329f323410] are everywhere now, many of them with features that generate nude images and videos of real people.
