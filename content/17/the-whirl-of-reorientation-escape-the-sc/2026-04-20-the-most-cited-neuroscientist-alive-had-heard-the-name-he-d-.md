John Boyd spent the last twenty years of his life working on a problem most people couldn’t see clearly enough to even name. How does a living system — a pilot, a company, a military unit, a market — stay current with an environment that never stops changing? His answer wasn’t a framework. It wasn’t a consulting model. It was a sketch. Nonlinear. Orientation-dominant. Built from Gödel, Heisenberg, and the Second Law of Thermodynamics.
Most people who’ve encountered Boyd’s work have encountered a reduction of it — a four-box cycle taught as a speed formula in leadership retreats and military staff colleges. Observe. Orient. Decide. Act. Faster. That version leaves out everything that made Boyd’s insight worth having.  
We have been arguing for years, on this platform and on the No Way Out podcast , that Boyd’s actual sketch  — the one he drew once, around 1995, two years before he died — describes something far more significant than a decision-making cycle. It describes how living systems process reality. How the internal model shapes what gets observed before any conscious deliberation begins. How the organism acts to test hypotheses against the environment, not to execute steps in a sequence. The capacity for free and independent action is the end goal, and orientation quality is the variable that determines whether you get there.
Last week, we spent two hours and fifteen minutes recording with Professor Karl Friston. 
If that name is not immediately familiar: Friston is a Professor of Neuroscience at University College London, a Fellow of the Royal Society , and the most cited living neuroscientist on the planet. Over ninety percent of all published brain imaging studies use the statistical tools he built. Beyond that, he is the architect of the Free Energy Principle and Active Inference — a formal mathematical account of how living systems stay alive by minimizing surprise. His work is cited across neuroscience, psychiatry, artificial intelligence, philosophy of mind, and financial markets. If you have read anything serious on consciousness, psychedelics, peak performance, or AI in the last decade, his name is in the footnotes.
He had never seen Boyd’s sketch.
We spent the first hour walking him through a visual model of his own framework — element by element, no equations — and asked him to explain each piece in his own language. The fitness landscape. The boundary between organism and environment. The internal model. Prediction and prediction error. The simulation loop. The reflexive action pathway. The deliberate planning pathway.
Then we showed him John Boyd’s OODA “loop” sketch.
His reaction was not a polite acknowledgment. It was recognition. Immediate and specific. He identified it as addressing the exact misconception he spends his career fighting — the idea that cognition flows left to right, step by step, phase by phase. He named the circular causality. The non-linearity. The dominant role of orientation. He said Boyd’s framing of decision as hypothesis and action as test was, in his exact words, isomorphic with active inference.
That was the opening.
What followed — on orientation failure, on the two kinds of surprise that determine competitive advantage, on why large language models cannot act in any meaningful sense, on markets as open systems that cannot be modeled with equilibrium physics, on the formal mechanism behind why expert intuition fails — is what paid subscribers access below.
This is a record of an independent convergence of a retired U.S. Air Force colonel working from thermodynamics and mathematical logic in the 1970s, who drew a sketch in the 1990s, and the most cited neuroscientist alive, working in 2025 on statistical physics and Bayesian mechanics, described the same structure without having seen it.
The convergence is independent. That is what makes it matter.
