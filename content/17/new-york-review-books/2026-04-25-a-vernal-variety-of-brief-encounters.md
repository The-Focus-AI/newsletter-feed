# A Vernal Variety of Brief Encounters

**From:** The New York Review of Books <newsletters@nybooks.com>
**Date:** Sun, 26 Apr 2026 04:09:55 +1000
**Source:** [View original](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-ahhiukl-36F2A313-iruduywlh-hk)

---

Sponsored by  The National Library of Norway [https://www.nb.no/fosse/lecture-and-prize/]

As spring slowly settles into the northern hemisphere, we have now published an auspicious thirteen conversations with writers as part of the Review’s weekly “Brief Encounters” interview series, on subjects ranging from  life in Gaza [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2025/10/25/what-day-after-for-gaza/] and  the West Bank [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/evil-in-the-west-bank-david-shulman/] to  the allure of gemstones [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/12/all-that-glitters-cartier-gems-and-the-new-science/],  the sting of satire [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/all-of-us-yahoos-state-of-ridicule-dan-sperrin/], and  the rowdy art of the Middle Ages [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/28/indecorous-decorations-medieval-sexuality/] to  ballet [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/alexei-ratmanskys-leap-of-faith-the-art-of-the-fugue/],  taxonomy [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/26/call-me-by-your-names-every-living-thing-jason-roberts/], and  Arundhati Roy [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/26/mother-trouble-mother-mary-comes-to-me-arundhati-roy/].

Below we have collected the interviews from the year so far alongside the articles that inspired them.

Sara Roy                  Policies of Denial [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/01/03/policies-of-denial-sara-roy/]

“What I sense most powerfully from my friends in Gaza is a feeling of abandonment.”

What ‘Day After’ for Gaza? [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2025/10/25/what-day-after-for-gaza/]

The most influential plans for rebuilding Gaza start from the premise that Palestinians have no right to determine their future.

Victoria Chang                  Community Poetry [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/01/10/community-poetry-victoria-chang/]

“I’m interested in writing my way through, around, above, and below my life, as a way to navigate life’s joys, beauties, and sadnesses all at once.”

The Swan, No. 20 (Hilma af Klint) [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2025/10/23/the-swan-no-20-hilma-af-klint-victoria-chang/]

The canvas is flipped from right to left. But the shell is smaller.

All morning I thought the shell was the same shell. That it was a

seashell. But maybe it’s a snail shell. I knew my placement of the

shell on the beach couldn’t have lasted. Now my mind must move. In

our lives how many things are like the snail, never thought of?… [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2025/10/23/the-swan-no-20-hilma-af-klint-victoria-chang/]

Helen Epstein                  In the Despot Archives [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/01/17/in-despot-archives-helen-epstein/]

“Government officials routinely distort the truth, incite ethnic hatred, treat the legislature and the media with contempt, and appoint partisan judges, while security forces carry out arbitrary arrests, torture and kill people, and run amok in other countries.”

Uganda’s Two Tyrants [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/01/15/ugandas-two-tyrants-idi-amin-yoweri-museveni/]

Idi Amin and Yoweri Museveni both confronted, in different brutal ways, the challenges of governing a postcolonial nation.

Jenny Uglow                  Sparkle and Status [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/01/31/sparkle-and-status-jenny-uglow/]

“Biography is a wonderful way into the past, because it’s life as experienced, day to day, subtly influenced by what is happening in politics or the movement of ideas.”

All That Glitters [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/12/all-that-glitters-cartier-gems-and-the-new-science/]

The science of gemstones has always been intertwined with their value as luxury items.

Alma Guillermoprieto                  The Writer from the Dance [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/02/07/writer-from-the-dance-alma-guillermoprieto/]

“The twentieth century’s great utopian experiments are exhausted, but there doesn’t seem to be an alternative vision on the horizon that people might want to sacrifice for and struggle to achieve, and the wreckage all around is great.”

A More Pliant Chavista [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/12/a-more-pliant-chavista-venezuela-guillermoprieto/]

President Trump’s decision to support Delcy Rodríguez as Venezuela’s new leader makes clear that oil, not democracy, is his main concern.

Advertisement

Vivian Gornick                  Home Free [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/02/21/home-free-vivian-gornick/]

“Every writer, sooner or later, must face the fact that our characters are taken directly from our own lives, so there will be friends, relatives, and acquaintances who are going to feel like they’ve been pushed under the bus.”

Mother Trouble [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/26/mother-trouble-mother-mary-comes-to-me-arundhati-roy/]

In her new memoir, Arundhati Roy tries to find the language to grapple with the shadow of her formidable, extraordinary mother.

Ian Tattersall                  For the Fossil Record [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/02/28/for-the-fossil-record-ian-tattersall/]

“I took the opportunity to observe the surviving lemurs in their natural habitats—and it was love at first sight.”

Call Me by Your Names [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/02/26/call-me-by-your-names-every-living-thing-jason-roberts/]

The quest to fathom the riotous diversity of nature is absorbingly told in a virtual double biography of the great taxonomist Carl Linnaeus and his contemporary, the count of Buffon.

Marina Harss                  En Pointe [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/07/en-pointe-marina-harss/]

“I’m struck by ballet’s ability to create something extraordinarily beautiful out of something so difficult and so taxing on the brain and body.”

Alexei Ratmansky’s Leap of Faith [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/alexei-ratmanskys-leap-of-faith-the-art-of-the-fugue/]

Having wrested himself from Russia after the invasion of Ukraine, the great choreographer has sought to remake himself and his work in Denmark.

David Shulman                  ‘Like a Gossamer Sheet’ [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/14/like-a-gossamer-sheet-david-shulman/]

“The first time I experienced life on the West Bank, staying over in Palestinian homes, a whole new horizon opened up for me. I entered into that life, its personal friendships, its language, its ravishing landscapes, and its evident suffering. All of it felt meaningful and real.”

Evil in the West Bank [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/evil-in-the-west-bank-david-shulman/]

As long as the daily horrors in the occupied territories continue and the extreme right remains in power, democracy in Israel will be sick at the core.

Advertisement

Erin Maglaque                  Spirit in the Sky [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/21/spirit-in-the-sky-erin-maglaque/]

“Religion could be a source of catastrophic oppression for women as well as offering a way to imagine freedom.”

God’s Impertinent Prophets [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/gods-impertinent-prophets-voices-of-thunder-baker/]

A new history brings to light the dissenting women who wrote, preached, and testified during England’s tumultuous seventeenth century, claiming the standing to speak as excluded outsiders who had an unfiltered knowledge of God.

Aaron Matz                  Novels of the Future [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/04/04/novels-of-future-aaron-matz/]

“To ask what literature has been doing all this time, as atmospheric carbon dioxide has been rising to ever more alarming levels, is to ask about the basic task facing writers.”

All of Us Yahoos [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/all-of-us-yahoos-state-of-ridicule-dan-sperrin/]

A new history of satire wants to limit the genre to its political ramifications, but satirists are often interested in the whole person and their capacity for vice.

Adam Hanieh                  A Widening Gulf [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/04/11/a-widening-gulf-adam-hanieh/]

“It would be a mistake to treat the Gulf as politically homogeneous. The war has clearly shown the weight of Saudi Arabia and the UAE, but it has not eliminated the different calculations of other Gulf states.”

Bottling the World Economy [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/23/bottling-the-world-economy-hormuz-gulf/]

Now that the Gulf states have become diversified industrial giants, blocking the Strait of Hormuz will have catastrophic effects on trade networks and food systems across the globe.

Lauren Kane                  After the Mystics [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/04/18/after-mystics-lauren-kane-medieval-art/]

“There’s nothing quite like the captivating feeling of coming upon, say, a faded relief on a stone wall in some unassuming place, and finding that the work is so much better than it has to be, has a mastery of skill or an originality of thought beyond its purpose.”

Indecorous Decorations [https://www.nybooks.com/online/2026/03/28/indecorous-decorations-medieval-sexuality/]

Medieval ideas about sex and love, both rowdy and reverent, are recorded on objects meant for everyday use.
