Free from the Archives

John James Audubon was born 241 years ago today, in Saint-Domingue. In the Review’s December 31, 1964, issue, some years before a larger reckoning with his legacy as a slave trader and sometime fabulist unfolded in Audubon societies and popular discourse alike,  Eleanor Clark wrote about the significance of his contributions [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1964/12/31/audubon-the-last-days-of-nature/] to art, taxonomy, and the historical record. In particular, she noted, Audubon’s painstaking cataloging of biodiversity in the United States stood in opposition to “the full horror and nastiness of the ‘visual pollution,’ ‘visual mayhem’—current newspaper phrases—and the other mayhems and pollutions being perpetrated on our land and therefore on our lives.”

Eleanor Clark

Audubon: The Last Days of Nature [https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1964/12/31/audubon-the-last-days-of-nature/]

Through and over everything was the fierce double drive of his mission, part art, part ornithology, and as immense and exacting in one respect as the other. It was the ruling principle long before his failures at everything else gave him the nerve to acknowledge it. “If I live to complete it, I will offer to my country-men a beautiful monument to the varied splendor of American nature.”
