—Russell-Dad Whiting [ https://substack.com/profile/6268785-russell-dad-whiting?utm_source=substack-feed-item ]
I believe this is the article you have in mind, Russell? The Roots of our Political Violence?   Relevant, yes; salient, partly; all wet, no—but very overstated. Not worthless, but overstated in ways that matters.
The article is good and original, but what’s good isn’t original, and what’s original isn’t good. The good part is the familiar but important observation that American reform politics has often drawn on Protestant revivalist energies, especially in the nineteenth century. The original part is Doran’s effort to inflate this into a grand, monocausal theory of American political pathology—a sweeping genealogy of modern progressivism as quasi-Gnostic, proto-totalitarian revolt against nature. That’s much less persuasive. 
It is absolutely true that American reform movements tend to draw on Protestant revivalist energies. The Second Great Awakening, the Burned-over District, abolitionism, temperance, women’s rights, perfectionism, millenarian sects, communitarian experiments, spiritualism, Mormonism, Adventism—all draw or drew from the same religious and emotional reservoir. So if the question is, “Is there something about American political culture that repeatedly turns politics into moral crusades?” then yes. Absolutely. That’s salient.
But Doran isn’t just saying, “American politics reflects this Protestant moral intensity.” He is saying something more like, “Modern progressivism is a post-Christian, quasi-Gnostic, Puritan political religion whose logic runs from abolitionism through social justice to metaphysical rebellion against nature, and ultimately to violence.” And that argument is tendentious. It treats very different movements—abolitionism, feminism, contemporary social justice activism—as if they were stages in one unfolding metaphysical disease. History is far more complicated than that. 
It’s absolutely true that the United States has never been a fully secular political culture in the European sense. (And neither has Europe, really:  It’s just buried religion a bit deeper .) American politics has always absorbed religious longings—you repeatedly see the themes of covenant, chosenness, jeremiad, redemption, apocalypse, purification, national mission. The Burned-over District really did produce a dense thicket of reform movements and religious experiments. It’s perfectly fair to say that the moral imagination of evangelical Protestantism shaped abolitionism and other nineteenth-century reform causes. It’s also fair to say that American politics often turns policy disputes into dramas of sin, redemption, and damnation. 
But the fact that abolitionism and the movement for women’s rights emerged partly from revivalist reform culture doesn’t mean they were pathological. Abolitionism was morally correct. Women’s rights were morally correct. The presence of religious intensity around a cause tells us something about its style, not its truth value. That’s the article’s sleight of hand: It treats fervor as evidence of fanaticism, and fanaticism as evidence against the cause. But some injustices deserve fervor. Slavery wasn’t a minor administrative defect in the republic. It was a monstrous institution. Those who failed to be morally inflamed by slavery were morally defective.
John Brown is a harder case. He really was violent and millenarian. He really did help precipitate the Civil War. But to make Brown the hidden ancestor of modern progressivism is a polemical flourish, not an argument. Brown is noteworthy precisely because most abolitionists didn’t become John Brown. 
If you want to explain political violence in America, you can’t just trace a line from revivalism to abolitionism to contemporary progressivism. You have consider slavery itself, white supremacist violence, frontier violence, anti-Catholic violence, labor violence, nativist violence, lynching, paramilitary groups, anarchism, state violence, and the myth of redemptive violence across the American political spectrum—which has many intellectual forefathers. And you have to consider nihilism, too: Hardly a Protestant sentiment. As I wrote here, there’s an eerie similarity between America’s culture of mass shootings and the sinister and contagious form of violent nihilism that emerged between 1861 and 1866 in Russia:
Orestes Brownson is an interesting figure, and his suspicion that humanitarian reform could become a substitute religion isn’t trivial. There’s a serious conservative insight there: Modern politics can become soteriological. Once politics promises redemption, it easily becomes coercive, because anyone standing between humanity and salvation becomes not a political opponent but an obstacle to be removed.
But this idea has to be handled carefully. Brownson opposed slavery, but he also feared the radical implications of egalitarianism. His anxiety about “obliterating all distinction of sex” isn’t prophecy in some simple sense; it’s also nineteenth-century conservative alarm at women’s emancipation. That controversies over sex and gender make Brownson sound “prescient” to Doran doesn’t mean his whole theory was right. You can oppose utopianism without treating every expansion of equality as a revolt against creation. 
Similarly, his invocation of Legutko. The Demon in Democracy is a polemic against liberal-democratic modernity. There are some perceptive observations there about conformism in liberal societies in that book, but its totalitarian-democracy thesis is much too broad. Liberal democracies can become illiberal, censorious, and morally self-intoxicated. But saying they therefore have the “same” totalitarian potential as undemocratic societies flattens distinctions that matter enormously. 
The word “Gnostic” is also doing too much work. Calling modern progressivism “Gnostic” has become trendy among highbrow conservatives. Sometimes it illuminates. But more often it just cloaks the writer’s disdain in metaphysical language. “Gnostic” functions, in this article, as an accusation: Progressives aren’t just wrong; they’re engaged in heresy. That might be rhetorically satisfying, but only if you don’t ask too many questions.
Also, the article conspicuously underplays the right-wing and reactionary forms of political religion. If we’re talking about salvation through politics, millenarianism, martyrdom, sacred violence, and the fusion of Caesar and God, we can’t just ignore contemporary Christian nationalism. Or militia movements, QAnon, replacement theory, Flight 93, and the cult of providential strongmen. You can’t plausibly use the Burned-over District to explain progressive fanaticism while ignoring right-wing movements entirely. Doran is making the typical polemical mistake of noticing the tendency he deplores only in people he dislikes.
The better point is broader and less partisan: Politics in America have always been vulnerable to religious transposition. But that’s not a uniquely progressive temptation. Nor is it a uniquely American phenomenon.

I don’t like the Southern Poverty Law Center. It treats perfectly legitimate views as if they were evidence of extremism; its ideological categories are sloppy, self-flattering, and defamatory. Ayaan Hirsi Ali’s treatment by the SPLC was a disgrace. 
But the Trump administration’s legal campaign against it is outrageous. And Hirsi Ali’s applause for it is an equal and opposite disgrace. 
The maneuver is now all too familiar. First, a senior official brandishes a sheaf of official-looking papers before the press corps. Then comes the solemn announcement that the documents prove a vast and sinister political conspiracy. The assumption—always sound, alas—is that no one will read them. The document has performed its function merely by existing. 
Tulsi Gabbard did this with her Russiagate folder: Here, behold, proof that Barack Obama engineered a fiendish coup plot against Donald Trump. The documents, for anyone deranged enough to read them, proved no such thing. But the point was never proof. A folder was waved. Words were intoned. The desired audience received the desired conclusion.
The SPLC indictment is the same trick. In their press conference, Todd Blanche and Kash Patel accused the SPLC of “manufacturing the “extremism it purports to expose” and fueling “racial hatred” in the US.  “The SPLC is manufacturing racism to justify its existence,” said Blanche.
The casual consumer is meant to absorb the following story: The SPLC paid racists, therefore the racists were not real; the watchdog created the wolves; the anti-extremism industry invented extremism so better to fund itself. 
But the indictment says nothing of the sort.  It does not allege that paying informants is a crime. It could hardly do so, since the federal government itself pays informants with the enthusiasm of a Victorian philanthropist funding orphanages. The FBI, drug-enforcement agencies, counterterrorism units, police departments, shady journalists, private investigators, fraud examiners, and intelligence services all rely on sources, some of them unsavory. That is why they are useful. People with pristine moral hygiene are rarely embedded in neo-Nazi networks. They tend to be at farmers’ markets, selecting the least problematic radish.
The actual allegation is narrower: that the SPLC used fictitious entities and misleading bank paperwork to conceal payments to field sources. That may be a legal problem. If false statements were made to federally insured banks, if business records were falsified, if accounts were opened under sham entities with the intent to deceive, then the question belongs in court. Fine. Litigate it. But the leap from that allegation to “the SPLC invented white supremacy” is not an argument. It’s a propaganda hallucination.
Indeed, the indictment’s own narrative depends on the reality of the extremist groups involved. It refers to the Klan, neo-Nazi networks, Aryan Nations affiliates, National Alliance circles, and Charlottesville-adjacent organizers. These aren’t figments summoned into being by direct mail. The administration wants to take the SPLC’s alleged use of sources inside extremist movements and turn it into evidence that the movements themselves were somehow fictive. By that logic, the FBI’s use of informants in organized crime would prove that the Mafia was invented by the Bureau.
The legal question is whether the SPLC concealed payments improperly. The political story you’re being sold is that white supremacy itself was a civil-rights fundraising scheme. Those are not the same proposition. They are not even close relatives. The difference is the difference between “the detective used informants” and “therefore the crime wave was staged by the detective.” 
The donor-fraud theory is especially rich. We’re asked to imagine SPLC donors discovering, to their horror, that an organization they funded to monitor violent extremists had paid sources to penetrate violent extremist networks. One can picture the shock: “I gave money to help expose neo-Nazis, and you spent it finding out what neo-Nazis were doing?” 
The government’s theory depends on a bizarre picture of what SPLC donors thought anti-extremism work consisted of: newsletters, seminars, earnest reports, perhaps a tote bag? Any serious donor to an organization monitoring violent extremist networks would surely understand that such work requires sources, infiltration, intelligence, and ugly contact with ugly people. That’s not a betrayal of the mission. It’s the mission. The notion that donors were defrauded because SPLC money was used to obtain intelligence from inside the movements SPLC existed to monitor is, shall we say, not the strongest limb on which the government has ever perched.
Elon Musk, as usual, has supplied the interpretive service for people who find reading too austere. His contribution is meme laundering: taking a narrow indictment about alleged bank-account concealment and converting it into the sweeping allegation that white supremacy was a manufactured civil-rights product. His role in this little pageant is to perform illiteracy at scale. The government waves an indictment; Musk reads the press release; the press release reads the political desire; and the desired conclusion is then blasted to hundreds of millions of people as if it had survived contact with the document.
Donald Trump, of course, has derived the obvious and logical conclusion, as any rational man who is wholly fit to control our nuclear arsenal would:
What makes the performance especially grotesque is that the SPLC worked with the FBI for years. The FBI evidently found the organization’s information useful until Kash Patel decided, in the new dispensation, that the real national-security threat was not extremist violence but the people who monitored it. This doesn’t immunize the SPLC from bank-fraud law. But it does make the theatrics somewhat difficult to swallow. Yesterday’s useful source network has become today’s sinister conspiracy, because the political weather changed.
Again: I don’t like the SPLC’s ideological habits. But the SPLC’s flaws aren’t a license for the government to manufacture a false story about them, and by implication civil-rights work itself. There’s a profound difference between saying, “This organization has sometimes smeared people unfairly,” and saying, “This organization invented white supremacy to fleece its donors.” 
The administration gets away with these outrages because citizens don’t read.  They announce what the papers “prove,” then rely on repetition, tribal appetite, and the public’s learned helplessness before legal language. The indictment becomes an icon, and its contents are almost beside the point.
The SPLC may have questions to answer about paperwork, banking representations, and operational concealment. The administration has a different project: to persuade the public that the watchdog created the wolves. That’s not law: It is its opposite. And theater for the functionally unread.

—George Hawrysch [ https://substack.com/profile/8196947-george-hawrysch?utm_source=substack-feed-item ]
George, I hesitate to divulge the full regimen, but since you ask: It is very simple. In infancy, I was placed on the standard JS Mill schedule, though my parents improved upon it by eliminating play. Greek began at eighteen months; Latin at two; moral philosophy at two and a half; and by three I was expected to produce a defensible position on the Corn Laws before being allowed a biscuit. 
At four, I was allowed to use blocks to compare the Peloponnesian War with the War of the Spanish Succession. I was thereafter given the Russian canon, the Ricardian theory of rent, and a small harpsichord upon which I was permitted to express no emotion not previously authorized by Burke. At five, I was introduced to Ottoman tax farming, modal logic, and the works of Carlyle; I was soon enough started on Sanskrit—chiefly to prevent me from developing vulgar emotional attachments in English.
The piano was deemed too sensual, so I was assigned fugue analysis. Calculus was permitted only after breakfast, provided I had first declined five irregular Hebrew verbs and identified three errors in Gladstone.
To maintain the apparatus now, I rise before dawn to perform ten repetitions of “what is the category error here?” and fifteen minutes of source criticism. I  mentally prosecute three public figures, then enjoy  a cooling stretch in which I contemplate civilizational decay. Breakfast consists of coffee, a boiled egg, and three paragraphs of prose by someone both wrong and pleased with himself. I am bolstered by the invigorating discovery, renewed each morning, that the world is governed by men who would have struggled with the simpler passages of The Blue Book.
At midmorning I take my constitutional, arguing silently with Lacan, Saussure, or the latest American to mistake a character disorder for a theory of government. Luncheon is followed by a restorative period of resentment, ideally converted into prose before it becomes medical. In the afternoon, I rotate among perceptual frameworks: history, theology, strategy, and the study of power as exercised by people who should never have been allowed near a microphone. Davidzon is not, regrettably, drilling me in the Russian canon. (He is too preoccupied with his doomed love affairs.)
As for the garden: It is not so much a garden as an overeducated allotment. There are trained hedges of history, espaliered Renaissance shrubs, a poisonous patch of Middle Eastern acronyms that no known herbicide can kill, and several beds of resentment; there is a special plot reserved for Fungoids: strange growths, natural and wild, many-hued, exquisite, full of poisons, and privately printed. The poetry grows badly but survives neglect. The strategy section is overrun by despair. The literature bed is healthier, though Nabokov has strangled several smaller plants. In the corner, an Oxford shrub keeps muttering “define your terms.” 
Compost is essential. Into it go bad metaphors. With luck, by spring, some of it comes back as usable copy.
I admire your piano and multivariable calculus. I may respond by taking up chess problems, or the fitted sheet, which remains the Everest of domestic topology. One does what one can.

QUESTION: Can your bots collect the nuts of data discontinued, distorted and suppressed by the US government into some sort of comparison with such operations by other authoritarian states, like Russia? 
—Paul Janos
Paul, I’m guessing—forgive me if I’m mistaken; if I am, tell me—that you mean to ask whether the AI tools to which I have access can gather the fragments of US government data that have been deleted, discontinued, altered, ideologically recoded, or made harder to access, and compare that pattern with the way authoritarian regimes such as Russia suppress or manipulate information. Is that correct? 
If so, the answer is yes, but the comparison would need to be precise. The United States isn’t Russia. American public data systems are still monitored by courts, watchdogs, universities, archivists, journalists, state governments, FOIA litigation, and a large independent research ecosystem. But that, perhaps, is why the comparison is useful. Democratic decay doesn’t begin with a Ministry of Truth. It begins with broken links, changed definitions, missing baselines, politicized categories, chilled agencies (or agencies axed entirely), and statistical series that quietly stop being comparable over time.
There’s already a great deal to examine: the removal or alteration of federal climate, demographic, and public-health resources since 2025, for example, and the legal fights over restored health datasets. Librarians, researchers, and civic groups are scrambling to preserve endangered federal data.
A CG piece could compare several mechanisms: deletion, category manipulation, funding strangulation, intimidation of expert agencies, replacement of neutral terminology with ideological language, uncertainty about official numbers, or the creation of rival official narratives. The Russian comparison would be useful not because the systems are identical, but because Russia shows the endpoint. Once public statistics become instruments of regime legitimacy, citizens no longer know whether they are reading information or state propaganda. 
My guess is that you’d find that America is not much like Russia. But regimes like Russia’s illustrate the mature form of practices that begin, in democracies, with bureaucratic vandalism and ideological cleansing. I don’t know that AI would be necessary to conduct an inquiry like this. It would just take a lot of time, and a serious budget. 
The US is not yet an authoritarian state. Most democracy indices consider it a flawed or partial democracy. The Economist’s 2025 Democracy Index   assigned it a score of 7.65, which is below the 8.01 threshold for a full democracy, but nowhere near 3.99 (a score below that denotes an authoritarian regime). It has presumably sunk even more since then, but I doubt it’s even fallen into the “hybrid” category yet. It’s been in the “flawed” category, by the way, since 2016; since Trump’s re-election, it has fallen from 28th to 34th.
The difference between “flawed democracy” and “authoritarian regimes” is very significant.

QUESTION: As I look at the problems of humanity, many (most?) seem of the multipolar trap variety. And, as the population careens toward ~10 billion, I think the only way to mitigate these traps is through stronger global institutions. (I’m just a dumb engineer, but I do have a lot of experience in control of complex, nonlinear systems—not unlike human ecosystems?) Do you agree? Is this possible? Are we doomed?
—Blake Suhre [ https://substack.com/profile/18032714-blake-suhre?utm_source=substack-feed-item ]
Blake, by “multipolar trap,” do you mean problems that arise when multiple actors, each behaving rationally according to the incentives they face, produce collectively disastrous outcomes? I’m going to assume that you do, because as it happens, this kind of problem is, in my view, precisely the kind that most threatens humanity right now: Climate change, arms races,  AI competition, tax competition, pandemic failure, antibiotic resistance, migration pressure, cyber conflict, and perhaps even social-media epistemic collapse all have exactly this structure.
So I’ll assume you mean situations in which no single actor can solve the problem by behaving well, because each actor fears being punished for unilateral restraint. If one state decarbonizes while others free-ride, if one laboratory slows AI development while rivals accelerate, if one country maintains humane asylum rules while others dump the burden, if one platform refuses to permit inflammatory content while competitors monetize it, the system punishes virtue and rewards defection. I’ve written about this problem before:
I’m instinctively sympathetic to the argument for stronger global institutions. This is not because I fondly imagine a benevolent world government issuing edicts from Geneva in immaculate sans serif—and even if I did, the phrase “world government” alone is enough to make half the planet reach for a rifle. I’m sympathetic to it because there’s no alternative. Many of our most urgent problems are beyond the capacity of the nation-state, acting alone, to solve. The great political dilemma of the twenty-first century is that the most serious dangers we face are planetary, but political legitimacy remains national—or even tribal.
The engineer’s analogy is useful. Human societies are complex, nonlinear, adaptive systems. They have feedback loops, time delays, tipping points, perverse incentives, cascading failures, and actors who learn how to game the control mechanism. But the analogy should serve as a caution against technocratic fantasy. You can’t control a political ecosystem the way you control a machine, because the components have beliefs, identities, resentments, memories, and lawyers. Any institution strong enough to solve global collective-action problems must also be constrained, lest it become a problem in its own right. 
So, do I agree with you? Yes. Emphatically. We need stronger global coordination, stronger monitoring, stronger enforcement, and stronger capacity to produce public goods. In the upcoming US presidential campaign, I’ll be looking carefully to see which candidates understand this and say so. 
But the phrase “global institutions” needs to be defined carefully. Sometimes this means formal treaties; sometimes clubs of major powers; sometimes standards bodies; sometimes insurance pools; sometimes data-sharing systems; sometimes scientific early-warning networks; sometimes sanctions regimes; sometimes boring institutions that nobody’s ever heard of but everyone depends on. 
The tricky question isn’t whether we need stronger institutions (we do), but what kind of strength those institutions need—informational, legal, fiscal, coercive, technical, reputational, norm-setting? The World Health Organization, the International Atomic Energy Agency, the WTO, the IPCC, the Financial Stability Board, the Montreal Protocol regime, aviation safety systems, and nuclear nonproliferation architecture all exercise very different kinds of institutional power. Some work better than others, because the underlying incentive structure is different. Looking at the UN General Assembly will give you a good idea of what doesn’t work. 
“Stronger global institutions” has to mean mechanisms for escaping particular traps, designed specifically for the institution in question,  not vague paeans to multilateralism. Climate, AI, pandemics, migration, financial contagion, nuclear risk, and information warfare aren’t the same problem. They don’t solve identically. The world can’t be governed by one magnificent global parliament. It needs to be carefully patched by hundreds of partially overlapping regimes that make defection harder, cooperation cheaper, and catastrophic error less likely.
Let’s consider an example that’s been at the forefront of my many preoccupations in recent years—AI risk. This is a textbook trap of the kind we’re discussing: enormous upside, strategic rivalry, private profit, military utility, opacity, speed, and a huge potential catastrophic downside. It’s a better pilot case than “global governance” in the abstract, because the relevant actors are (relatively) identifiable—frontier labs, major cloud providers, chipmakers, governments, militaries, standards bodies, and a finite number of research ecosystems.
We have no need for a vague Ministry of the Future to manage this problem. We need something like the IAEA. But it has to be adapted to a technology that’s  private, fast-moving, commercially valuable, and dual-use from inception. 
The institution’s purpose should not be to “govern AI” in some vague Davos sense. It should be to govern frontier AI capabilities that create systemic risk, and as a first sketch, it probably ought to have four jobs:
Registration and monitoring. Any actor training or deploying frontier models above a defined compute, capability, or autonomy threshold would have to register them. That includes model architecture basics, training compute, major datasets at a high level, safety evaluations, deployment context, and incident reporting.
Evaluation and licensing. Systems wouldn’t be deployable just because a company built them. They would require pre-deployment testing by accredited evaluators or by the institution itself, looking especially at the potential for cyberattacks, autonomous replication, biological-design assistance, persuasion or manipulation at scale, weapons integration, and loss-of-control indicators.
Tracking compliance. Because models are hard to see but large-scale compute is visible, the institution should monitor the supply chain for advanced chips and high-end cloud clusters—the AI equivalent of watching fissile material. This is an imperfect way to do it, but much more feasible than watching every line of code.
Emergency coordination. The institution would maintain a crisis mechanism in the event of dangerous capability breakthroughs, leaks of frontier weights, autonomous agent failures, or evidence of state or military misuse.
The closest analogue would be the IAEA, which offers a model for the way it could conduct inspections, establish safeguards, and verify that dangerous capabilities aren’t being diverted into prohibited uses. It could also resemble the Financial Stability Board, in that it would identify institutions whose failure or recklessness could endanger the whole system, or the International Civil Aviation Organization, which sets technical standards in a way that might be worth emulating (states retain sovereignty, but common standards make the system safer). And it could be modeled on the OPCW, too, which declares whole categories of weapons beyond the limits of international tolerance, and the IPCC, in that it would issue periodic, authoritative, consensus statements about risk.
The institution I have in mind would have to borrow from all of them. No single template already exists, because AI isn’t just an industry, but it isn’t quite a weapon, isn’t quite infrastructure, isn’t quite speech, and isn’t quite a scientific field. It’s all of them. 
The hard part, as always, is legitimacy. A global AI regulator would immediately face objections. The US will be afraid of constraining its own firms while China races ahead.. China will fear that AI safety is a disguise for American technological containment. Companies will fear losing trade secrets and the advantage of speed. The rest of the world will fear being permanently locked out of a technology developed elsewhere and then regulated by those who already have it. 
So this institution would need a bargain at its core, a bit like the bargain that made the NPT successful. (Reasonably. So far.) Access to the frontier AI ecosystem comes with obligations: disclosure, testing, incident reporting, liability, and compliance. In return, states and firms gain legitimacy, access to shared science, protections like insurance, compute markets, and maybe preferential access to certified models. This part really matters. A regulator that only says “no” will be evaded. A regulator that genuinely creates a safer, more trusted market will be seen as useful.
What would “strength” mean in this context, then? Not world government, obviously. It wouldn’t mean blue-helmeted AI inspectors kicking down the door at Anthropic. It would mean that the organization has the following capabilities: 
Eyes: The institution is able to see where the biggest training runs and deployments are happening.
Standards: It defines common testing protocols for dangerous capabilities.
Verification: It has the power to audit claims, inspect facilities, and demand access to the logs.
Leverage: It can deny access to chips, cloud compute, public contracts, insurance, and international deployment permissions.
Speed: It has emergency powers to convene governments and labs during an incident.
Public trust: It publishes enough to make the world less dependent on corporate self-certification.
Are we doomed? I don’t know. We have a very dangerous institutional lag. Our problems have globalized faster than our capacity to govern them. The optimistic case is that humans have repeatedly built institutions after discovering that unmanaged competition was intolerable (central banks, public-health systems, arms-control regimes, aviation rules, maritime law, nuclear hotlines, ozone agreements). The pessimistic case is that we tend to build them only after catastrophe has done the teaching. We have to learn before the exam is administered with live ammunition. 
Also, the power that would once have naturally led the campaign to create these institutions—the United States—has gone berserk. I don’t know if it retains the capacity for rational behavior. It certainly no longer has the moral authority that leading other countries requires. Americans are now profoundly hostile to international institutions of any stripe. In some cases, the hostility is well warranted (see: UN Human Rights Council ). In others, it’s absurd. 
Furthermore, to use the example of my hypothetical AI-regulation institution, our tech companies are now more powerful than our government. I think you’re asking whether political history is cyclical, whether technological evolution is linear, and whether AI breaks the old cycle by giving cyclical human beings irreversible powers. Is that right? If so, my answer is: yes, that is one of the central dangers. Human passions recur; technology accumulates. We keep replaying old dramas with more powerful tools.
I don’t ascribe to a simple cyclical theory of history, though I understand the attraction of one. Political history plainly has recurring forms: oligarchy, demagogy, imperial overstretch, revolution, decadence, reform, restoration, war, exhaustion, renewal. Everything’s a footnote to Plato.
But technology isn't cyclical in the same way, because it accumulates. Human beings stay recognizably human, but the instruments available to human folly become more powerful. That’s why I would never say history is a circle. It’s closer, as you point out, to a helix: recurring human patterns under conditions of increasing technical capacity. 
Politics is cyclical because human passions are recurrent; technology is cumulative because knowledge compounds. The crisis of modernity (an inherently pretentious phrase, but what can I do, that’s what it is) is the collision between those two facts. We keep reenacting ancient dramas—fear, pride, rivalry, resentment, utopianism, greed—but with instruments of ever-increasing power. So I think your premise is partly right. The same patterns recur, but at higher energy levels, so to speak. 
AI matters because it may raise the energy level so dramatically that the old mechanisms of correction—defeat, embarrassment, bankruptcy, institutional reform—come too late. Institutions evolved for human-speed politics may be overwhelmed by machine-speed finance, propaganda, warfare, surveillance, and scientific discovery. If the old cycle was ambition, overreach, collapse, recovery, the new danger is unrecoverable overreach.
So I take your question very seriously. It’s at the center of most of what I write about these days. We may have finally built tools powerful enough to destroy the ecological and political conditions under which, previously, civilization renewed itself after episodic catastrophes. 
Still, I’d resist the fatalism. As I often say, you mitigate existential risk the same way you mitigate any other risk: by being more careful. I said it here, for example:
Our species has built restraints before: law, taboos, arms control, constitutionalism, scientific norms, insurance, central banks, public health, aviation safety, nuclear command protocols. We can do it. But can we build new institutions fast enough to keep cumulative technology from turning recurrent human stupidity into terminal human stupidity? That’s the question. 
The phrase “biological evolution” is, I think, doing too much work in your formulation. Evolution isn’t a command to “compete till death.” It also produces cooperation, kinship, reciprocity, symbiosis, restraint, and social intelligence. The problem isn’t evolution as such. The problem is that human beings evolved under conditions radically unlike those created by modern technology, and we’re not well-made for a world bursting with nuclear weapons, viral engineering, algorithmic propaganda, and artificial superintelligence. We have Stone Age threat responses and tribal cognition. Now, we have godlike instruments. A regrettable product launch.
The alternatives aren’t necessarily “Eden or glass.” Eden isn’t coming back. Annihilation isn’t inevitable. There’s a plausible future somewhere between paradise and vitrification: a long, dangerous struggle to domesticate our own capabilities. If we succeed in building sufficiently robust institutions, perhaps we can keep our ancient primate impulses from using modern tools to turn the planet into a memorial to our cleverness. 
We should certainly try.
