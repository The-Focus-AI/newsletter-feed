Oracle’s deluge of AI debt pushes Wall Street to the limit.

Oracle’s $300 billion megadeal with OpenAI is testing the limits of Wall Street’s appetite for debt tied to America’s data-center boom. Banks including JPMorgan Chase struggled for months to spread the risk of billions of dollars in loans they made to build Oracle-leased data centers in Texas and Wisconsin, report Peter Rudegeair and Berber Jin. Many financial institutions that would ordinarily buy those loans face restrictions on how much exposure they can have to a single counterparty, and the sheer size of these debt packages  pushed them to the limit [https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/oracle-ai-demand-debt-04977749] with Oracle. This clogged bank balance sheets, constraining the financing prospects of future projects tied to Oracle and OpenAI. The challenges surrounding Oracle highlight a risk for the multitrillion-dollar data-center boom.

A breakthrough heart procedure comes with risky tradeoffs.

A transcatheter aortic valve replacement procedure is considered one of the biggest innovations in cardiovascular medicine, offering a way to spare patients the physical and emotional trauma of open heart surgery. Yet there’s limited research on how long the valves might last. And as TAVR has become more widely used among younger and healthier people,  some are finding [https://www.wsj.com/health/healthcare/heart-valve-tavr-surgery-aorta-1e0eda70] that their valves don’t work as well or last as long as they hoped. The procedure they thought would spare them a complicated surgery leads some to the operating table anyway, reports Betsy McKay.

Q: The Trump administration has reclassified marijuana as a less dangerous drug. But what are the health risks?

In many states, it is already easy to get marijuana. With the Trump administration’s move to reclassify the drug as less dangerous, it is about to get even easier. But doctors and researchers say marijuana  can pose real risks [https://www.wsj.com/health/what-you-need-to-know-about-the-health-risks-of-marijuana-06b5a5d5] to people’s health, explains reporter Andrea Petersen.

A: The major concerns for adults are addiction and mental-health problems. These risks have become more of an issue in recent years as products with high levels of THC, the main psychoactive component of cannabis, have become widely available and popular.

The weed that people smoked in decades past generally had about 3% to 5% THC. Now, many shops sell products that contain as much as 90% THC.

Dr. Jonathan Avery, vice chair for addiction psychiatry at Weill Cornell Medicine, says he’s seeing more people land in the emergency room after accidentally overdosing on high-potency THC products, particularly edibles.

While many people use cannabis to cope with anxiety, some studies show that the drug makes anxiety worse. It is associated with increased odds of developing anxiety and more severe symptoms in those with anxiety and mood disorders.

Doctors and scientists tend to worry most about marijuana's impact on the developing brain. Research has found that adolescent cannabis use increases the risk of psychosis, bipolar disorder, depression and anxiety disorders.

One potential upside to reclassification: The move will make it easier for researchers to conduct studies with marijuana, something scientists say the field badly needs.
