EXPERT TAKE

Q: What’s it like to retire in America after a divorce?

While national divorce rates have hit historic lows for younger generations, the rate for older Americans has climbed. Several people spoke to reporters Veronica Dagher and Anne Tergesen about their  post-divorce retirement lives [https://www.wsj.com/personal-finance/divorce-retirement-america-6d03e3e2].

A: For many Americans, their golden years are being rewritten by divorce.

These late-stage splits often upend retirement plans, forcing people to navigate expenses with halved 401(k)s and little time to shore up their savings. That’s all while facing the daunting task of rebuilding their lives. Yet many end up finding a sense of freedom in their newfound independence. We spoke with four retirees about how they successfully navigated life after divorce:

Mark Sutton, 74, spends $240,000 annually. He told us that his divorce in 2014 slashed his net worth. He and his former wife divided his pension and his $1.1 million 401(k). He also paid her for her share of the value of their home, which he still lives in. To make up for the financial setback, Sutton postponed his retirement from age 65 to 70.  Lynn Meinor, 70, spends $86,000 annually. She told us that when she realized she might be heading for a divorce three decades ago, she made a key decision with her retirement in mind. Instead of taking a job in the private sector, she joined the Utah Department of Health & Human Services. A big draw was the state’s pension.
