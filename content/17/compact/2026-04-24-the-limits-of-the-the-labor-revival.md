> Mutiny: The Rise and Revolt of the College-Educated Working Class
> By Noam Scheiber
> Farrar, Straus, and Giroux, 384 pages, $32

In 1912, dozens of Harvard students skipped their exams in order to help break up one of the biggest labor confrontations of the early twentieth century: the International Workers of the World (IWW)-led textile workers’ strike. All union-busting students received an automatic Gentlemen’s C on their midterms. For these students, this represented not just a chance to get college credit for making trouble, but to assert their class privilege. 

Since the foundation of the United States, a university education has been a ticket to the ranks of the economic, political, and social elites. That meant most college grads saw their economic interests aligned with those of big business, not the labor movement. But the days when a bachelor’s degree served as a guarantee of economic security are long gone. Starting in 2022, the unemployment rate among college graduates exceeded the national rate. And by some accounts, the growth of AI is keeping it high. In March, The New York Times reported that it could be the worst spring since the pandemic for recently-degreed job seekers. Unlike their counterparts a hundred years ago, many of today’s students and recent graduates are throwing their support behind the labor movement. The transformation of this once-privileged demographic is the focus of Noam Scheiber’s Mutiny: The Rise of the Revolt of the College-Educated Working-Class. 

The declining value of a college degree is to a large extent a matter of supply and demand. The rapid growth of the college-educated population—from just 17 percent in 1980 to nearly 40 percent today—means a bachelor’s degree doesn’t mean as much as it used to. According to the Cleveland Fed [https://www.compactmag.com/r/2c1c7dea?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637], the college-wage premium—the difference in earnings between those with and without a bachelor’s degree—has declined by 10 percent. 

It’s likely to get worse. “Assuming sustained growth in the employment share of college-educated labor and a continued lack of skill bias in technological progress,” said report authors Barış Kaymak and Alexander Cline, “we project a decline in the college wage premium over the coming decades.” The share of college graduates still living at home has climbed [https://www.compactmag.com/r/6cc13135?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] steadily since the dawn of the millennium, and remains historically high. Then there is debt. College costs have tripled [https://www.compactmag.com/r/38c8950a?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] over the last fifty years, while the debt balance per borrower rose 39 percent [https://www.compactmag.com/r/09846fcc?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] from 2008 to 2022.

While Mutiny contains many stories about professional and personal disappointment among striving Gen-Z jobseekers, Scheiber’s emphasis is on how these struggles are forging a new activist vanguard in the working-class—from low-level Hollywood writers to coffee shop workers—that has turned to collective bargaining to remedy their problems. His subjects aren’t just passive victims of a broken social contract, but rank-and-file leaders of some of the biggest labor actions in recent years. He profiles a Watson Fellowship recipient turned Starbucks barista and strike leader; Towson University graduates organizing their Apple store co-workers; degree holders turned warehouse workers who scored labor’s first organizing victory at Amazon. 

Scheiber argues that the turn to labor by college-educated workers isn’t mainly driven by political idealism, but by a response to real economic insecurity—the same impulse that drove auto workers and steelworkers to organize more than nine decades ago. There is some debate about just how deprived college graduates are in relation to non-degree holders. Despite the real financial struggles faced by degreed Zoomers, Eric Levitz [https://www.compactmag.com/r/e0f0445f?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] at Vox argues that, by some metrics, graduates are economically doing better now than they did in the nineties, citing data showing they are less likely to be underemployed or to hold a low-wage job than in years past. 

This wouldn’t be entirely surprising. Historically, labor organizing has been spurred less by poverty than disappointment. The Homestead steel strike of 1892 was driven in part by independent craftsmen who found themselves less and less essential in the face of technological shifts—something Hollywood writers and video game designers competing with AI can relate to. The rise of the Congress of Industrial Organizations in the 1930s was driven in part by workers’ frustration with the collapse of welfare capitalism. Promoted by employers in the 1920s, welfare capitalism had been developed to keep unions out of the workplace by offering benefits like health care, educational opportunities, and even company unions where workers could raise grievances.  

By the time the Great Depression hit, workers realized those benefits could be easily taken away. Working meat packers or steelworkers were in a relatively privileged position compared to those still on the unemployment line or the millions of destitute rural dwellers. But they had been raised to believe they deserved more, and that gave them the appetite to fight.  

Scheiber’s subjects likewise share a sense that the ladder has been pulled up from them, and that the promises made to them by economic and political elites are hollow. Not coincidentally, two of the companies most prominently featured by Schieber—Starbucks and Apple—in many ways resemble the welfare capitalist firms of the twenties. Compared to your average fast-food chain, Starbucks offered competitive health and educational benefits, including full-tuition coverage for a first-time online bachelor’s degree from Arizona State University. Apple store employees weren’t just clerks; they were, in Apple parlance, “creatives.” Both companies promoted a progressive image, supporting a range of social justice issues from LBGTQ rights to Black Lives Matter. 

> “For left-leaning college grads, they promised a better world.”

For left-leaning college grads, these workplaces promised a better world. But corporate progressivism showed its limits when confronted with workers’ demands for a fairer workplace. As Scheiber recounts, Starbucks founder Howard Schultz’s meticulously constructed progressive image collapsed when workers demanded independent representation. Like Henry Ford and Andrew Carnegie before him, he took this as a personal insult. In addition to targeting union supporters for discipline and firing, the company hired one of the nation’s largest union-busting firms [https://www.compactmag.com/r/fdbeda68?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637], while showering non-union stores with new benefits unavailable to union ones.  

----------------------------------------

 

The rise of the credential proletariat has brought a new energy to the labor movement. The bigger question is whether it can translate that energy into a wider working-class project. So far, that remains unclear. The degreed left has found a political voice. Economically frustrated college grads have propelled politicians like Zohran Mamdani and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez into office and fueled the rapid growth of the Democratic Socialists of America. 

But there is little evidence that democratic socialism, no matter how populist in rhetoric, has much appeal with the non-degreed working class. Scheiber tends to underestimate the problem, quoting the left-wing writer Thomas Frank to the effect that all the Democrats need to do to win back working-class Trump voters is take economically populist positions. 

But the truth is that left-wing populists running under the Democratic label have found it hard to attract non-college educated voters. Mamdani struggled with voters without a degree. In 2020, Elizabeth Warren ran on an ambitious New Deal-inspired economic platform, but tanked [https://www.compactmag.com/r/667380c4?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] with blue-collar America. Nationwide, nearly 70 percent [https://www.compactmag.com/r/760b9a77?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] of voters without degrees say the Democratic Party has moved too far to the left. This isn’t an electoral problem for Democrats in many major cities, but it is one in states and districts where degree holders are a minority of the electorate. 

> “Labor’s revival will remain a revolt of downwardly mobile graduates”

Compared with most of the last century, the gap [https://www.compactmag.com/r/c1aa0a19?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] between college- and non-college-educated voters has narrowed on economic issues. From higher taxes on the rich to support for labor unions, there is broad support for an economically populist agenda. But that hasn’t produced a political coalition that bridges the educational divide. Because the gap on issues like immigration, crime, and social priorities remains wide, it can’t be papered over by policies like a higher minimum wage.  

Unless the college-educated labor left can translate its own sense of frustration into a project that gives voice to the concerns and demands of those on the other side of the college divide, labor’s revival will remain a revolt of downwardly mobile graduates, not the working class as a whole. And forging that coalition may prove more challenging than facing down Howard Schultz or Tim Cook.
