Now, on to Ancient Philosophy for a Chaotic Modern World…
Where do I belong? What is my purpose? What is the point of it all?
If those questions sound familiar, then congratulations! You’re having the same existential crisis as the ancient Greeks during the era of Hellenism and the rise of Alexander the Great.
You see, they had previously been insulated in their city-states, safely cocooned in a shared sense of identity and purpose, carefully crafted by their local polis.
Then, not quite all of the sudden, their world become much... bigger... interconnected... and frankly more confusing.
I know. Hard to imagine.
Faced with this rapidly expanding, multicultural world, the average ancient citizen found themselves staring down some uncomfortable questions about who they were, why they mattered, and where exactly they fit in all this newfound chaos.
Enter the Stoics.
These philosophers stepped up to fill the growing philosophical void, offering guidance, structure, and a helpful hand in a world that no longer felt quite so steady.
Now, we can argue about how successful these men were in spreading Stoicism across the ancient world, but one thing is beyond debate: Stoicism has aged exceptionally well. Perhaps that’s because the reasons it mattered then are eerily similar to the reasons it matters now.
So today, dear reader, we’ll dip our toes into Stoicism itself before diving headfirst into the profound (and surprisingly practical) ideas of its greatest thinkers.

What Is Stoicism, and Where Did It Begin?
First things first, what is Stoicism? 
Broadly speaking, Stoicism is a school of philosophical thought that arose during the Hellenic age of ancient Greece. Zeno of Citium (334 – 262 BC) is typically credited as the founder of Stoicism.
The term “Stoicism” comes from the Greek word “stoa” which refers to a colonnade typically built outside or inside an ancient temple. Zeno of Citium was said to have lectured at the stoa poikile (painted colonnade) in ancient Athens, and thus, his followers were referred to as “Stoics”.
The various tenets of this philosophy were crafted and adjusted over several centuries. Eventually, Stoicism would find great favor in the Roman Empire, with several notable Roman intellectuals and leaders who were, at the very least, influenced by Stoic philosophy. Cicero, Seneca, and Emperor Marcus Aurelius all espoused the ethical precepts of Stoicism.
