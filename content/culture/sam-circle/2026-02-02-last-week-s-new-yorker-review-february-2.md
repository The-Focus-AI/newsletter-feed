[**‚ÄúA Fatal Error‚Äù (A Reporter at Large)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/did-a-celebrated-researcher-obscure-a-fatal-poisoning?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Ben Taub milks it for all it‚Äôs worth.** An instant classic, but pitched wrong: Starting with pathos and the dead baby does mean that the reveal of Koren‚Äôs true nature is a twist, but it makes the story seem like a downer, something to be braced for. # **Window-Shop:**

[**‚ÄúMoon Shot‚Äù (Letter from Japan)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/how-shinzo-abes-assassination-brought-the-moonies-back-into-the-limelight?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- E. Tammy Kim aids and Abes.** I appreciate that Kim doesn‚Äôt bother moralizing about Abe‚Äôs assassination, which was a minor tragedy with a major impact, single-handedly changing the public perception of Moonies in Japan. This won‚Äôt be the most surprising piece if you‚Äôve followed the contours of the Abe assassination and are generally familiar with the Moonies (whose brief [acquisition of Newsweek](https://www.theguardian.com/media/2014/mar/28/newsweek-new-owners-background?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2) doesn‚Äôt even get a mention here ‚Äì sorry to our friends at _Last Week‚Äôs Newsweek Review_) but Kim keeps things moving. This piece is prompted by the assassin‚Äôs trial, but the trial barely concerns the state corruption it unveiled, so she relies on journalist Eito Suzuki, whose blogging helped inspire the assassin, to fill in these details; Kim spends too much time on his personal story, though, and never quite manages to make him feel central. [**‚ÄúStone Face‚Äù (Books)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/island-at-the-edge-of-the-world-mike-pitts-book-review?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Margaret Talbot is head of state.** Easter Island is a remote speck with an artistic heritage so innately appealing it‚Äôs taught to schoolchildren; it is both predictable and unfortunate that the local islanders were dismissed and speculation as to the Aryan giants who _must_ have built the statues ran rampant. I knew about the absurd Kon-Tiki voyage, which takes up too much time here, but a new book carefully documents not only the clear case that the island‚Äôs cataclysm was a result of enslavement and disease rather than environmental degredation, but also that we ought to have _known_ this to be the case for quite a while. Instead, pioneering research was lost (due apparently to tragic happenstance) and primary sources were overlooked (probably for more maleficent reasons), while the Kon-Tikis of the world took over the narrative. The corrective is a bit self-evident, but it‚Äôs not uninteresting, and the heads have, of course, their own appeal. See, for instance, [this poem](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2016/10/03/resolve-poems-nyla-matuk?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2) the magazine published a decade ago; it‚Äôs stuck with me. üóø!

[**‚ÄúEmpire of Quiet‚Äù (Musical Events)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/morton-feldman-at-100-music-review?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Alex Ross wants no Morton is necessary.** The eerie, ‚Äúmonkish‚Äù minimalist Feldman gets a neat tribute here, though it‚Äôs too bad that, as Ross points out, there aren‚Äôt any big celebrations in the city of his birth. Instead Ross eats cake in L.A, focusing a lot on the specifics of performance in ways that are difficult to parse without hearing what‚Äôs being referenced. I‚Äôd have rather spent longer in suspended motion with Feldman, and less time rushing through the awful present.

[**‚ÄúLong Story‚Äù (Books)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/infinite-jest-david-foster-wallace-anniversary-book-review?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Hermione Hoby has a fantod limn**[**.**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phantom_limb?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**** The backlash to the David Foster Wallace backlash has been brewing for a while, and Hoby is here to pitch in. Her attempts at homage, which mainly involve the use of some _eleven_ -dollar words among this magazine‚Äôs usual surfeit of tenners , are not convincing1, and her indulgence in bemoaning the writer‚Äôs apparently trite cancellation _before_ pointing out that, actually, _plenty_ of young and youngish women are huge fans, is tiresome. If you‚Äôre looking for a brief yet coherent review of an extremely difficult book to review with coherence yet brevity, though, you could do much worse than the second section. And the examination of Wallace versus a previous generation of white male novelists ‚Äì whom he hated! ‚Äì is insightful, even as I have quibbles with it. (Hoby‚Äôs [been mislead on the subject of gooning](https://www.thechatner.com/p/but-pornosexualgooner101-died-35?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2); the five long novels by women she cites are all hundreds of thousands of words shorter than _IJ_ , which really doesn‚Äôt help her point, though interestingly she does _not_ cite [a certain infamous novel by a woman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_Shrugged?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2) which actually _is_ longer2; she also references the apparent underacnowledgement of Don DeLillo‚Äôs influence ‚Äì how could anyone possibly acnowledge his influence _more?_!3) She finally suggests that many Wallace haters are probably just people that watched [that one insipid graduation speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eC7xzavzEKY&utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2) in high school health class, and she even cites the anecdote‚Äôs far subtler and more ambiguous appearance in _IJ_ ; the comparison to George Eliot, whose work was given her day‚Äôs equivalent of the brainyquote.com treatment, is apt, and as a letter of recommendation for a book that is also a project, this does its job. Come on in, the water‚Äôs unnoticeable.

[**üó£Ô∏è ‚ÄúLove Language‚Äù (How-To Dept.)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/how-to-woo-with-words-alone?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Dan Greene gets a personal loan.** The ratio of three somewhat unbearable normies (‚Äúa publishing-house tote‚Äù, ‚Äò‚ÄúSpain-late‚Äù‚Äô!!) followed by a genuine weirdo ‚Äì someone who, in just a few lines of dialogue, conveys a bracingly strange existence; he is something like a Zoomer R. [**Nussbaum on Kramer (Takes)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/takes/emily-nussbaum-on-jane-kramers-founding-cadre?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**-** Always a compelling subject, but this feels so edited-down it only barely coheres. Nussbaum isn‚Äôt just covering the initial, rather long piece on consciousness raising (including unveiling every single pseudonym for no clear reason), but also a follow-up, partly a mea culpa, written by Kramer decades later, and _also_ more recent cultural engagements with women‚Äôs groups! # **Skip Without Guilt:**

[**‚ÄúFake News‚Äù (American Chronicles)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/tucker-carlsons-nationalist-crusade?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Jason Zengerle knows bow tie is in the eye of the beholder.** Zengerle is a new staff writer at the magazine, and his first piece here is excerpted from his also-new book on Carlson. Not sure why this isn‚Äôt labeled a profile, as that‚Äôs its form to a T; it paints Carlson as an embittered opportunist, something that will be pretty self-evident to anyone who‚Äôs watched the guy for ten seconds. He‚Äôs self-evidently repugnant, and his slide toward conspiracy and open fascism is the result of deliberately putting a thin faux-intellectual veneer on whatever stance happens to be winning at the moment, or, even better, looks like it might be winning in a few clicks. Zengerle doesn‚Äôt get any big scoops ‚Äì he‚Äôs not able to nail down the reason for Carlson‚Äôs firing from Fox ‚Äì and although he keeps things moving (or, whoever edited this down from book length did), he can tack a bit simplistic in the process; he‚Äôs too focused on making Carlson look _weak_ rather than evil. It affirms the alpha/cuck dichotomy that Carlson, Nick Fuentes and his ilk obsess over. We can do better than that.

[**‚ÄúCounternarratives‚Äù (The Current Cinema)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/natchez-movie-review-mr-nobody-against-putin?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Richard Brody sees the revision.** These movies both sound totally compelling, but I don‚Äôt need a confusing Brody plot synopsis of either, which is all he‚Äôs brought; evidently his style clashes badly with the needs of a documentary review, since deep analysis doesn‚Äôt fit his (laudable!) singular-vision fetish. The latter is probably for the best (I don‚Äôt need a point-by-point breakdown) but this hardly serves as more than a thumbs-up stamp.

[**‚ÄúMeet the Press‚Äù (On Television)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/the-presidential-press-conference-spin?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Vinson Cunningham puts a**[**stick**](https://idioms.thefreedictionary.com/put+a+stick+in+my+spokes?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**in the spokespeople.** I generally enjoy Cunningham‚Äôs media criticism, but we should at this point stop doing character analyses of Trump. It‚Äôs been a decade of this shit; we all understand who the guy is. I guess Cunningham‚Äôs point is that Caroline Leavitt is a top-notch spin artist, so if the public is turning against Trump, it‚Äôs not a matter of his having inept publicity? Post-X, Post-[Bezos‚Äô blog](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/30/nx-s1-5692923/washington-post-bezos-layoffs?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2), et al, it‚Äôs impossible to claim any mass-media bias _against_ the guy without outing yourself as part of his cult. So Cunningham is left arguing a point that‚Äôs self-evident.

# **Shut the Fuck Up:**

[**‚ÄúOne Direction‚Äù (Annals of Politics)**](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/what-maga-can-teach-democrats-about-organizing-and-infighting?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**\- Charles Duhigg says shit can get**[**litter than lit**](https://genius.com/Pusha-t-if-you-know-you-know-lyrics?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)**mus.** (His numbers don‚Äôt add up on the polls; that was [ten years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2), if you know, you know.) Duhigg is a decent tech reporter with a sideline in [pop-economics books for dumbass technocrats](https://www.amazon.com/dp/081298160X/?ots=1&slotNum=0&imprToken=1abf9466-8554-35fb-d35&tag=thneyo0f-20&utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2), and he‚Äôs here to tell us what the Democrats are doing wrong. Wouldn‚Äôt you know it? He‚Äôd like them to copy the MAGA movement, operate more like a business, and, yup, lighten up about all that _abortion_ and _gay rights_ stuff so as to build a ‚Äúbroader‚Äù coalition. This is nasty business, as will hopefully be self-evident to the intelligent readers of this newsletter, but man does it come across as especially noxious at the very moment when Minnesotans (and plenty of Americans elsewhere) are demonstrating what _actual_ solidarity looks like. [Adam Serwer‚Äôs article](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/2026/01/the-neighbors-defending-minnesota-from-ice/685769/?gift=SNRqQQTCF6y4GFvKBlylyzwkgAKkhiCJBBM6UvdYzF4&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share&_bhlid=5bcc9e19d975809dcc679df3085d366c05aa5667) in _The Atlantic_ is an especially useful corrective; he‚Äôs previously lead the charge to accurately label Trumpism as a politics of [cruelty and exclusion](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/06/26/opinion/trump-republican-party.html?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2), whereas Duhigg apparently believes the Democrats are the exclusionary ones. But Serwer‚Äôs new piece is different: A superb chronicle of real-world resistance in Minnesota, it shows how diverse and cohesive communities work; that neighbors really _can_ protect neighbors, motivated by shared humanity and not by the MAGA movement‚Äôs handy gun clubs and homeschool group.4

I don‚Äôt think Duhigg gets _everything_ wrong here; his main case history is pertinent-ish, it‚Äôs just making a narrower point than he thinks. Yes, the 2008 Obama campaign correctly identified that the presidential election is easier to win when local campaigners aren‚Äôt rigidly bound to extremely limited talking points and tactics; the Tea Party subsequently took advantage of these techniques while the more hidebound Democrats squandered them. (There has long been a top-down lack of [daylight, kid](https://thehill.com/homenews/campaign/5191087-harris-trump-biden-harris/?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2).) But this is really _just_ a marketing lesson; it has little ideological significance, and almost nothing to do with the public disillusionment with Democrats more broadly. In fact, issues like abortion rights and LGBTQ acceptance ‚Äì things Duhigg hideously dismisses as ‚Äòlitmus tests‚Äô ‚Äì are (_beyond_ their obviously being core planks of the liberty and justice our country has long claimed to promote) also [_very popular_](https://static.foxnews.com/foxnews.com/content/uploads/2026/01/fox_january-23-26-2026_national_cross-tabs_january-29-release.pdf?utm_source=lastweeksnewyorker&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=last-weeks-new-yorker-review-february-2)5, even among the much-fetishised independent voters. More importantly, Duhigg‚Äôs annoyance that Democrats reject certain stances entirely ignores that the right rejects certain _people_! Duhigg likes MAGA because they have no ideology except blind faith and unity, but saying that this offers a lesson to Democrats is like saying Jonestown offers a lesson to party planners. Yeah, sure, _it worked for them._

I have to admit, this piece makes me furious, and it‚Äôs short-circuiting my ability to be concise and precise. Does Duhigg really think pluralism is such a liability it must be abandoned? Does he really think unity can be so easily engineered? Does he really see no hope for solidarity? Is he, maybe, just _afraid_?

It‚Äôs hard for me to read this as other than a piece born of deep cowardice, as Duhigg faces rising fascism and decides we can only beat the bully by joining him. Fuck that and fuck you. I believe that we will win!
