New Orleans the Long Way

by Kọ́lá Túbọ̀sún [https://flaminghydra.com/r/8140bcd1?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

“Let’s drive to New Orleans!” I said in February—an impulsive suggestion that had emerged not from contemplation or planning, but out of whimsy. In Minnesota, the trees remained dead from the weight of winter, and every few weeks the snow threatened to return, keeping the dream of spring away.

“You sure?” my wife asked.

I was going to New Orleans in any case: the Poetry Festival was happening there, as well as a screening of my film, and my flight from Minneapolis had already been booked for a scheduled trip of two days.

“Oh yes,” I said, thinking about it for the first time. “Why not? We haven’t gone on any road trips since we drove to Canada last summer, and it would be a nice change of scenery.” It would also be my wife’s first time in the city. My second.

“Okay then,” she said, and got to preparing the trip for a family of four in a rented minivan, as was custom. We’d be heading straight down the U.S.A. map from top to bottom, as if chasing the path of the Mississippi River itself [https://flaminghydra.com/r/37faa32f?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. Me in the driver’s seat, and the kids behind.

Cancelling my Delta flight was easy. Not so the Creole Inn, which I had previously booked for myself alone. My calls went to voicemail and I wouldn’t hear from anyone there until a day before check-in, when I had already made alternative arrangements for our lodging. 

Other things one didn’t plan for: the price of gas, or Trump’s debacle in the Persian Gulf. From less than $3 per gallon just over a month ago to almost $4, depending on the state, one felt the pocket drying up with every mile. Enterprise Rent-A-Car is happy to let you have the Chrysler Pacifica to go anywhere you like, as long as you bring it back with the tank half full, as it was when you set forth.

And so, through Iowa we went, with its corn fields and windmills; then Illinois, to sleep in Springfield, the capital, where I’d once explored the Lincoln Presidential Library and Museum as well as his burial place at Oak Ridge. The next morning, we drove through my old haunt, Southern Illinois University Edwardsville [https://flaminghydra.com/r/b47f5b62?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], to see an old colleague. The campus hadn’t changed much, though the university has taken steps to cut some departments [https://flaminghydra.com/r/c2debb94?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]—including my old one—on the pretext of some excuse about funding. Foreign Languages apparently don’t fit into the new administration’s idea of a moneymaker. Doug Simms bought me coffee and gave me a tour of the physical changes in the department where I once worked as a graduate assistant. A professor of German and Old English, he gave me my first job in the U.S. at the foreign language lab.

A few hours later, we were back on the road toward Kentucky. Time didn’t allow for a stopover on this leg of the trip, but on the way back I would take a small detour to collect a few of the famous bourbon and moonshine brands the state is known for. We eventually arrived in Tennessee for the night, a state we’d never been in before. The famous South.

Breakfast in Franklin, Tennessee was standard; the accent change of the servers was noticeable. Nashville was about twenty minutes away, but our late wake-up made it hard to do a proper detour to discover something of the city. We had plenty of miles to go, so onward to Birmingham we went, where the twang in colloquial speech became still more pronounced. 

My son’s search had suggested we check out the Birmingham Civil Rights Institute, a museum and educational center, and we arrived there late in the afternoon. The center showcases the history of Birmingham from its founding to its role in the Civil Rights movement, featuring life-size sculptures of famous figures; there were few visitors, at least at the time we went. 

Next, we put “soul food” in the map, which led us to Mama’ Nem [https://flaminghydra.com/r/86f6b0bf?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] on Lomb Avenue. We got our first taste of earthy and satisfying authentic Southern cuisine among a big crowd waiting in long lines. The sun was out in the sky and we were hungry. Black-eyed peas never tasted so good, sharing a plate with well-flavoured meat. My linguistic ear had its fill, too, of a new type of Southern Black English I hadn’t heard anywhere else. Musical, and intelligible to me only via close listening. I tried to limit my request to the server to repeat herself, but she was gracious enough to do so anyway. “I like your accents,” she remarked.

One more long drive through the rest of Alabama and Mississippi; at a Mississippi rest stop, we saw our first MAGA hat. It was on the head of an old white man, with a woman who looked like his wife holding his hand. Like us, they had stopped by the welcome center for a few minutes of rest, and they headed towards the bathroom which my son and I were also approaching. It was he who saw it first and he held me back, very scared.

“Oh that,” I laughed. We were far from the hearing of the couple. “We’re in Mississippi. I’m sure you’ll see much more before this trip is over. New Orleans is in Louisiana, which is where Mike Johnson is from.”

He, however, seemed genuinely terrified, as if the baseball hat itself was a sign that he was a target and currently in danger. He insisted we sit on a public bench until the couple had left. 

For someone who has never driven south of St. Louis, I never quite knew what to expect while driving south. But this hat was the least of my problems. Better to know who you’re dealing with as soon as you meet them than be rudely surprised later. Many friends who chose to live in the South have said the same: “Here, you know when people like you, so the relationships are genuine. In Minnesota, everyone is nice, but your neighbour will call the HOA if you leave your grass uncut for more than a few days.”

By sundown we were in New Orleans [https://flaminghydra.com/r/fdc60792?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122].

----------------------------------------

 

In conversation with residents of New Orleans I noticed frequent references to “Before Katrina” or “After Katrina”, which always carried the heavy weight of something really whimsical, poignant, or significant.

We visited the lively household of my wife’s cousin and her family, who live in the suburbs, with a few chickens, a cat, a boat, and an orchard in the backyard. Her husband showed me a map of New Orleans to show the proximate cause of the devastation that Katrina brought; it boiled down to the greed of the companies dredging the swamps for the benefit of their oil and shipping businesses. “It was inevitable that Katrina happened,” he said. “And it might happen again.”

He was raised in New Orleans, but his wife had grown up in Minnesota. They treated us to a dinner of gumbo, made with the Nigerian flavours of spice, okra, and peppers, but also stuffed with crabs and other delicacies of the city.

One of my university professors who lived here during the hurricane recalled in his book of poems [https://flaminghydra.com/r/55f47e44?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] how his family had to be rescued with a boat on the third floor of a neighbour’s building. “Katrina won’t get the last word,” he promises.

We visited City Park in New Orleans later, and the Museum of Illusions, a series of exhibitions that test your senses. There’s a room that appears upside down in mirrors, and a tunnel of colours that gives you the impression of being spun around even though the colours, whirling around you like a drum, are the only moving objects. There was a French Quarter festival happening, as the crowd near the water confirmed, so we went there, too. Then we went to Café Du Monde, where we had beignets and calas (delicious), among a throng of other tourists. 

The two-day New Orleans Poetry Festival itself was a success, though it took far less time than the trip. Everyone who heard we’d driven down from Minnesota had a version of the same exclamation: “That is thirteen hundred miles!” A weird bit of trivia to have in one’s head for unplanned colloquial deployment.

My colleague from Best Literary Translations, Wendy Call, was in town, as well as two contributors to this year’s edition: writer/translators Whitney DeVos and Chloe Martinez. Best Literary Translations is now in its fifth year. Arthur Sze, our guest editor, is the current U.S. Poet Laureate, and was interviewed in the New York Times that Sunday.

My film, Ebrohimie Road [https://flaminghydra.com/r/235a7d70?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], was screened at the Zeitgest Theatre and Lounge on St. Claude Avenue, run by the friendly, bearded Rene Broussard. Friends in attendance included Fẹ́mi Euba, a retired professor from the Louisiana State University and a subject in the film, and film professor Vanessa Uhlig.

And before we knew it, our time in New Orleans was over. 

----------------------------------------

 

On the way back, via Selma, we sought to see the Edmund Pettus Bridge [https://flaminghydra.com/r/c65d4c4f?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. It was a Sunday and the city was totally quiet. Not even restaurants were open. But the bridge remained, still bearing the racist’s name. The next day, at the U.S. Space and Rocket Center in Huntsville, we learnt a little more about the Space Program. The exhibition was extensive, and would have needed at least four hours to properly explore. The eighth day of the trip found us in Hannibal, where we visited the Mark Twain Boyhood Home and Museum (my fourth time there). 

In Trigg County, Kentucky, on the way back, I stared at the map on the wall at the Visitor Center, where the kind hostesses encouraged us to leave a pin signifying where we had travelled from.

We visited Metropolis, Illinois, where the giant Superman statue stands in the square downtown, made our way through Solon, Iowa, and followed the U.S. interstate through cornfields on the way back home, where I found that there had been an internet outage in our home all week long, so that the Ring camera I installed a few days before the trip had recorded nothing. 

It was a relief to be back in one’s bed in the end, we agreed, though my son insisted that heading back to school was a least-desirable outcome for him.

Perhaps a plane ride would have been better, if slightly more expensive than the $585.37 charged by Enterprise, though there’d have been much less to see of the country from above, arranged like pastry squares in a myriad of colours. When we add up the gas purchases, it all comes out pretty even.

“The body doesn’t quickly recover from such a trip though,” my mother-in-law quipped the next day, when I couldn’t get out of bed.

Still With the Band

by Tod Seelie [https://flaminghydra.com/r/a8649b8a?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

In my post-college years I went on a lot of tours with bands. I’m not a musician, but I am (or was, in a different era for my lower back) good at carrying heavy things, driving big vans, selling merch, sleeping under tables (one of my many tour hacks), and taking photos. I had a lot of friends in bands who appreciated the help, and who often had to hit the road, for example to SXSW in Austin, Texas. One of those bands was Parts & Labor [https://flaminghydra.com/r/df470872?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], a group of very nice guys with a very distinctive sound; the closest I can come to describing it is that they play noise like an instrument. It’s a powerful, full-force sound, and something you should really hear for yourself [https://flaminghydra.com/r/4719efd6?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122].

Fifteen years after their last release, Parts & Labor are back with an epic double album [https://flaminghydra.com/r/08fc6341?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]. I was fortunate to be asked to create their new band photo and greatly enjoyed coming up with a visual approach to complement their sound. I was able to steal them away one evening for a brief photo session in Joshua Tree during their recording session.

The album’s coming release and subsequent live shows got us thinking about “back in the day,” so I went and found photos from twenty years ago when I toured with them down to SXSW and back. Digital cameras and my photo skills were in their infancy back then, but they still strike me as fun and full of energy. It’s also amazing to see how much camera technology has progressed, and the way that these images look like the era in which they were shot: the style of the lighting, for instance, is just very identifiably from the early 2000s. 

Touring with bands could be complicated—bad food, bad tempers, long days and nights—but Parts & Labor were always enjoyable. I look back on my time with them as leveling up and getting to do it with the adults in the room.
