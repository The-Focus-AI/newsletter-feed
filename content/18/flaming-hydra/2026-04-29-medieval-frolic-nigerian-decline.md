Prithee, Faire Maiden

by Anna Merlan [https://flaminghydra.com/r/f38b505b?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

Near the end of my first and perhaps only Renaissance Faire, as I stood wilting in the heat and dazedly eating a frozen banana, a medium-size child crept up and quietly gave me the plague. She was maybe eight years old, her face hidden in a furry gray rat mask. She sidled up beside me and my friend and quickly stamped both of us with a drawing of a little flea. 

“You got the plague,” her mom called over. We clapped appreciatively as the little rat stealthily moved onto her next victim.

Irwindale is a flat and sleepy desert town in the San Gabriel Valley, about 20 miles east of Los Angeles. Here, in a mercilessly sunny park beside the Santa Fe Dam, we had come to attend the 63rd annual Renaissance Pleasure Faire, the nation’s oldest renaissance faire. The roasting-hot celebration is home to an absolutely flabbergasting number of people and things. Blacksmiths, archery ranges, a sword swallower, a “gnome hunt” for the children, hair-braiding “for maidens,” face-painting, juggling, jousting, children beaming in delighted confusion while being knighted; a young and grave Queen Elizabeth, in a heavy, beaded and embroidered dress and makeup that was probably lighter than historically accurate, being paraded through a solemn crowd; aerialists spinning themselves high into the air. My brain gently melted, from overstimulation and from heat, my flower crown slipping into my eyes. 

I strode through the gates amid a huge crowd that Saturday with my husband and two friends—the same people, in fact, who forced me to go to Disneyland [https://flaminghydra.com/r/8fe4adac?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] last year. We realized that one of our number needed to rent a costume, and stepped into the rental booth, where we dug through a bewildering array of corsets, dresses and various under-layers, and found one of each that suited her. 

“Hands behind your back like you’re under arrest,” a costume maiden said cheerily. She slipped the corset around my friend’s shoulders and then—there is really no other description for this—politely and with a great deal of force used the corset to hoist my friend’s bosom wondrously aloft. Now all suitably attired, we began to wander.

The first-ever renaissance faire is widely agreed to have been a backyard event in Los Angeles, which quickly became bigger and more elaborate due to America’s fever-dream fears of Communism. Its founder was Phyllis Patterson, a Los Angeles former school teacher who, as her son said, left teaching in part because she was forced to sign [https://flaminghydra.com/r/16f1ef6c?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] an anti-Communist HUAC-era loyalty oath. Patterson, who died in 2014 [https://flaminghydra.com/r/774881f9?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], taught English and drama, including a backyard program she ran at home in Laurel Canyon. Soon she began dreaming of setting up an Elizabethan-style fair; the first one was held in May 1963 at a local park, with public radio station KPFK as sponsor. 

According to the Faire’s official history page [https://flaminghydra.com/r/75658bd5?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122], McCarthyism almost brought the whole thing to a sudden stop. Patterson, “her abundant hair primly confined in a bun,” had to attempt “to convince conservative county officials to approve the educational value of the Faire.” 

“She testified repeatedly and patiently at public hearings about the whys and benefits of colorful costumes, beards, handcrafts, acoustic music, and artistic freedom in the outdoors,” the page continues, “introducing 1950s-style bureaucracies to the creative possibilities then manifesting in the early 1960s. Somehow, Phyllis always prevailed.”

 

[https://flaminghydra.com/r/d38c4511?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

The ’60s and ’70s medieval fashion vibe also took hold more broadly because, chiefly, it looked fantastic, especially on women, and served as a lushly countercultural response to the buttoned-up styles of the ’50s. Adding to the craze was Olivia Hussey, who played a very Ren Faire Juliet [https://flaminghydra.com/r/10f41b5c?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] in 1968: long hair, flowing nightgowns, and a gorgeous white brocade corset. Add to this the general social enthusiasm for nature—newly rediscovered, after the stifling, indoor, increasingly plastic-covered previous decades—along with occultism, flower crowns, and the still-inescapable specter of sensitive, acoustic-guitar-strumming young men. What we now call “medieval revival fashion [https://flaminghydra.com/r/5a2b42a8?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]” was everywhere, with the bright young things resplendent in velvet capes, bell-sleeve dresses, and, in extreme cases, a lute. 

To this day, somehow, that spirit still prevails. The Renaissance Pleasure Faire was—aside from one benighted and cranky-looking woman in a transphobic “Save Women’s Sports” shirt—is still very alternative, very freewheeling, and very visibly queer. 

“This is where the weird people are,” my Ren Faire-loving friend said contentedly. “The nerds are in charge.” It really is a huge cross-section of nerd types and fandoms, joined by their desire to play the game together; to leave our own terrible time, and live in another.

“Are there any gamers here?” a performer asked onstage at one point, leading to a huge round of cheers and applause.

Like attendees at Burning Man—but let it be said, less annoyingly than at Burning Man—we had entered a society with its own rules and customs. People knew to shout “huzzah to the tipper!” when someone tipped generously, and, at least in my observation, loved to hoot “Just the tip! Just the tip!” during a joust. At one point, a beautiful woman paused before me and gravely handed me a tiny plastic axolotl; exchanging trinkets is a recognized, if not officially sanctioned, part of attendance.  

As the day wore on and the heat began to overcome me, we ventured into a large patch of shade under a tall tree and lay down in the grass. I closed my eyes and kept them closed. I realized after a few moments that all I could hear were sounds that wouldn’t have been out of place 500 or so years ago: cheers, shouts, drumming, the jingling of coins and costumes, gasps of awe and amazement as an aerialist nearby did something brave and stunning. For a moment, I was, indeed, perfectly out of time and place.

STILL CRAZY OUT THERE

https://flaminghydra.com/r/f0572609?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122Luke O’Neil [https://flaminghydra.com/r/fdf5640d?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]https://flaminghydra.com/r/ae6325e6?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122Maria Bustillos [https://flaminghydra.com/r/4bf9639b?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]Hamilton Nolan [https://flaminghydra.com/r/b2679467?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

The Lucky Ones

by Jídé Salawu [https://flaminghydra.com/r/3bf5c103?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122]

https://flaminghydra.com/r/055c665e?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122

“Japa” is a Yoruba word that translates to “flee” or “escape” a precarious situation. But there is a specific context for the use of this word in modern Nigerian parlance. 

In 2020, Nigeria witnessed the nationwide #EndSARS protests [https://flaminghydra.com/r/9720d96f?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122] that led to the brutalization and state-sponsored killings of dozens of Nigerian youths in Lagos. The aftermath of this event led to an exodus of young people, who had seen the futility of their hopes for political change, so that today “Japa” has become a diasporic buzzword connected to the lack of faith in the Nigerian state and the country. The failure of the government to protect fundamental human rights is complete; the country has become a hardened police state where peacefully demonstrating puts your life in danger and can make you guilty of “treason.” This migratory wave was thus inevitable. It includes students and people in search of work, as well as political exiles who have had to flee for their lives. 

On X/Twitter the other day I noticed a post with the headline, “Japa blessings [https://flaminghydra.com/r/603d2b0c?m=e1004cfb-97ed-4594-9a60-f0bcd58e4122].” It gave me the impression of a Nigeria whose future is on a knife-edge. The poster spoke of the CAD 50,000 grant their sister had received in order to pursue her studies in Canada. This post celebrates the vast-sounding sum of money, but does not address the topic of the loans that students must repay upon completion of their programs. This contributes to the long tradition of grand narratives of diasporic success, consolidating the green-pasture mythology that drives the desires and fantasies of many Nigerians back home.

In 2021, after I left for Canada to pursue my PhD, my mother told me about someone who believed I had now “made it.” Made it in the Nigerian vocabulary, as in the U.S., is about material access, wealth, and fortune. To leave Nigeria means that you are up there ahead on the ladder. It earns respect for my mom and siblings, who receive a higher status because their son is now in Canada. It came as no surprise when my mom told me that this person asked if she is aware that I am now rich, just by the fact of having traveled to Canada. 

My mom, a migrant herself, understands the illusions brought about through stories of traveling and labor migration. So she replied, “How do you know he is making money over there? How are you sure he is not facing any challenge? Do you know what kind of work he does?” With this barrage, she confronted the myth of the green pasture. These are old stories, obviously, with many variations, including those in which migrants themselves exaggerate and embroider their realities to impress relatives back home, even when their lives have grown worse, and things are not working out for them. It’s very easy to spin the illusion that the diaspora is a migrants’ paradise, regardless of the stresses of life people may face there. 

There is a man in my hometown who traveled to America and returned without having made a fortune, which immediately suggested that something must be wrong with him. Gossip started about his having been “called” home by a spell, preventing him from accessing his large properties in the West. This man and his family are treated with suspicion, and their laughter is seen as a sign of the trauma of being expelled from a country like America. What in the world would make one leave the paradise of the West for a country riddled with poverty like Nigeria? Another colloquial phrase: the “been-to,” meaning one who has been educated in the West. That may not always work out, as in the figure of Baako, the African intellectual in Ayi Kwei Armah’s Fragments who, upon his return—and his subsequent failure to sort the material needs of his family and society at large—becomes a failed and disoriented figure.

By 2017, I had finished my master’s degree program and was very hopeful that I would have a chance in the academic world of Nigeria. But I planned to expand on my studies abroad, and in 2018 I had the chance to visit the University of Copenhagen in Denmark, and then I spent my Fulbright year at Fayetteville State University; after these experiences, my ideas about scholarship abroad had changed. It is undeniably the case that what makes a solid scholar is materials, networks, resources: world-class libraries, access to current knowledge, and to fellow scholars in one’s field.

My journey to Canada as a student started not only with the financial frustrations I faced during my master’s degree at Obafemi Awolowo University, but also with the absence of many resources that help make the quality of one’s scholarship stand out. I would have to ask friends based in America to help me access journals, books, and articles that my university could not provide. Even with excellent professors ready to assist students in their academic careers, the dilapidated state of the resources and facilities led me to change the direction of my research. It goes without saying that loans or grants for students are nonexistent in Nigeria. The schools run on a very tight budget, and the professors are also underpaid. With my migration to Canada, I was in a better place in terms of materials and resources. That is a privilege that millions of Nigerian students won’t have access to.

As a student in Edmonton, I know that the funding grants you access to all of this, and to resources for travel, and access to modest accommodation. I know that the loan I have received is not free money, but an infrastructural support for the dedication to my research. Not a blessing, but a contract, a set of obligations and responsibilities.

I have thought about this in the Nigerian context. In 2023, when Bola Ahmed Tinubu became President of Nigeria, part of his plan was to expand the tertiary education credit system through loans. This initiative met with fierce backlash, and it has generated its own set of problems. As with many political projects in Nigeria, the government was only interested in announcing the student loan budget. But with a sky-high rate of youth unemployment in the country, the prospect of creating jobs that will pave the way for repayment of the loans after school goes unmentioned. Equally concerning is the state of public universities in Nigeria, where libraries, laboratories, students’ hostels, and research programs and initiatives are almost universally underfunded. With these key facilities in such a deplorable state, the Nigerian education system is heading for ruin. And why not? The children of political elites get their education in the West, with no need for a scholarship. 

For the Japa student migrant, returning to Nigeria means a risky voyage home to a country in rapid decline, with zero investment in education or research. The Japa blessing, then, is a case of situational irony: your country is failing itself by not investing in education and knowledge, yet it continues to alienate and export its human capital. The migrant student, with his supposedly transformed life, returns from the diaspora to meet a mediocre and defeated population at home. The blessing is silent on the matter of the menacing future that awaits on their return.
