Q: How is the Senate’s new housing bill affecting builders and the rental market?

The legislation passed by the Senate in March is already causing  projects to pause [https://www.wsj.com/real-estate/a-bill-aimed-at-creating-homes-is-leaving-plots-empty-instead-c2569d83] and financing to dry up, explains reporter Rebecca Picciotto.

A: The bill is trying to make it easier to build more houses, but one of its provisions has stopped homebuilders in their tracks. It would force developers to sell, within seven years of completion, newly constructed homes that they built solely for the purpose of renting.

Investment in these so-called build-to-rent properties has quickly cratered. Developers say that investors won’t put money into new rentals that they can own for only a few years before having to sell them.

Already, at least $3.4 billion of investment for build-to-rent projects is frozen in place, according to an early survey of 14 build-to-rent firms. That translates to about 10,000 units of housing. And it’s just a sliver of the overall build-to-rent industry, which includes an estimated 1,700 firms.

The Senate bill isn’t yet law and some Congress members are pushing back. For now, the impact is a long pause of new construction of single-family rentals. But the longer the policy battle drags on, the more severe the damage to the build-to-rent industry.

Meet the Indian Ocean islanders who were pummeled by history—and now by Trump.            

PHOTO: MICHAEL M. PHILLIPS/WSJ

The people of the remote Chagos Archipelago have lived in limbo since being evicted in the 1960s and 1970s to create a buffer around the secret U.S.-U.K. military base on Diego Garcia. Then last year the exiles  thought they had caught a break [https://www.wsj.com/world/africa/the-indian-ocean-islanders-who-were-pummeled-by-historyand-now-by-trump-too-622004f4]: Britain struck a deal that would see the U.K. cede the islands to Mauritius and lease back the base, perhaps allowing the exiles to go home. But Trump seems intent on killing the deal, leaving hundreds of Chagossians worried they have lost their last shot at a life on the islands.
