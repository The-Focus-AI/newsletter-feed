Ahead of the 250th anniversary of the United States, WSJ is exploring the triumphs, trials and innovations that have shaped America and the world in a special multimedia series, “USA250: The Story of the World’s Greatest Economy.” In this special edition of The 10-Point, we’re taking a look at the evolution of innovation in America. This newsletter brings you some of our favorite pieces so far. You can  find all the coverage here [https://www.wsj.com/usa250].

            You ranked the most impactful U.S. inventions.            

PHOTO ILLUSTRATION: CLARK HODGIN FOR WSJ, AP, LIBRARY OF CONGRESS, BETTMANN ARCHIVE/GETTY IMAGES, CORNING

WSJ readers scored the country’s greatest breakthroughs, from the computer to the steamboat.  Here are the results [https://www.wsj.com/us-news/the-most-impactful-u-s-inventions-bf44040d].

            Business historians say these are the greatest entrepreneurs in U.S. history.            

ILLUSTRATION: ISRAEL G. VARGAS FOR WSJ

The top four names on the list—Henry Ford, John D. Rockefeller, Steve Jobs and Andrew Carnegie—aren’t remembered for optimizing stock prices, but for how they changed how people lived, worked, consumed and thought about tomorrow.  Read the full rankings [https://www.wsj.com/business/entrepreneurship/best-entrepreneurs-us-history-23c83c23].

            Information technology unified the nation.            

ILLUSTRATION: SHAWN MICHAEL JONES FOR WSJ

After America’s Revolution, slow communications made a lack of cohesion one of the biggest threats to the new nation. A series of technological revolutions—including the telegraph and the smartphone— made all the difference [https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/information-technology-us-4f3c71f3].

            The inventor dubbed “the Black Edison” beat Thomas Edison in court.            

PHOTO: ASSOCIATED PRESS

Granville T. Woods  won a series of patent battles [https://www.wsj.com/business/granville-t-woods-patent-battles-edison-6744bfcf] in the 1880s to protect his inventions, including a system to allow moving trains to communicate wirelessly.

            America’s ad business has come a long way since 1704.            

PHOTO: CHARLES PHELPS CUSHING/CLASSICSTOCK/GETTY IMAGES

As the U.S. grew, it helped turn sales pitches into an art—and an industry. Here’s a look at  some of the biggest moments [https://www.wsj.com/business/us-advertising-history-great-moments-418a8284] in America’s long and profitable relationship with sales pitches.

            Some American innovations were discovered by mistake.            

PHOTO: MATT CHASE FOR WSJ

Mistakes are in the DNA of the U.S.A.: Christopher Columbus was trying to find a westward route from Europe to Asia when he discovered the New World. Since then, the U.S. has repeatedly proved itself to be the land of luck, with these  10 U.S. innovations born of mistakes [https://www.wsj.com/business/us-inventions-mistake-discovery-8d0ff716].

            Meet the women who invented tech that transformed household chores.            

PHOTO: ARCHIVES CENTER, NATIONAL MUSEUM OF AMERICAN HISTORY, SMITHSONIAN INSTITUTION

American women have long played a central role in inventing  products that reduced the physical toil of daily housework [https://www.wsj.com/tech/women-inventors-household-gadgets-chores-9d431bac], though these innovations didn’t always make their inventors wealthier.

            These people didn’t actually invent what they’re famous for inventing.            

ILLUSTRATION: BEN HICKEY FOR WSJ

Long before Alexander Graham Bell patented the telephone, inventor Antonio Meucci created his own version. It takes an elusive mix of skill, resources and luck to bring an invention to the world—and history books are riddled with  pioneering inventors relegated to the footnotes [https://www.wsj.com/tech/us-famous-inventions-inventors-wrong-3d5b0a0f].

            See America’s top innovations in aviation.            

ILLUSTRATION: WSJ

From Kitty Hawk to the space program, entrepreneurs and the U.S. government have stoked a sprawling industry dedicated to getting humanity off the ground. WSJ surveyed  some of the major aviation innovations in U.S. history [https://www.wsj.com/us-news/from-kitty-hawk-to-space-see-americas-top-innovations-in-aviation-870e3213].

            Here are five tech innovations you could see in the next 25 years.            

ILLUSTRATION: ANDY POTTS FOR WSJ

WSJ asked five technology experts to  predict one great innovation [https://www.wsj.com/tech/ew-tech-expectations-expert-opinion-6e3e631d] they think we’ll see in the next two and a half decades. The world they envisioned includes mind-controlled devices and private weather control.
