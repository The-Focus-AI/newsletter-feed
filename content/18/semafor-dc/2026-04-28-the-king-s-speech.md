Eric Lee/Reuters 
Eric Lee/ReutersIt takes a lot to unite the Republican Party against an idea from President Donald Trump, but Spirit Airlines has accomplished the feat, Semafor’s [Eleanor Mueller, Burgess Everett and Nicholas Wu report.](https://semafor.com/s/ncuaFgnP3Z?email=wschenk@gmail.com) The idea of a government intervention or bailout of the troubled discount airline is getting panned from all corners of the party. “This would be a really bad idea. I don’t think you want the government owning airlines,” said Senate Majority Leader John Thune. Sen. Ted Cruz, R-Texas, called it “terrible,” Sen. Thom Tillis, R-N.C., called it “horsesh*t,” and Sen. Josh Hawley, R-Mo., said it’s “not his favorite thing.” An exception to the rule: Spirit Gold member Bernie Moreno, the GOP senator from Ohio, who said he shares Trump’s concerns about job loss and rising fares and it’s “not unreasonable” to have a conversation about Spirit.

↓↓22King Charles warns against looking ‘inward’

 
Eric Lee/Reuters 
Eric Lee/ReutersKing Charles III championed the US-UK relationship during an address to Congress this afternoon, stressing the need for cooperation on modern-day challenges. At a time of high tensions between the US and Europe, Charles touted bilateral trade and investment ties, and urged the US to remain in NATO. “I pray with all my heart that our alliance will continue to defend our shared values with our partners in Europe and the Commonwealth, and across the world,” Charles said to booming applause. “And that we ignore the clarion calls to become ever more inward-looking.” Earlier today, Trump and first lady Melania Trump welcomed Charles and Queen Camilla warmly at the White House. “Americans have had no closer friends than the British,” [Trump said](https://semafor.com/s/vfewxdr7yb?email=wschenk@gmail.com). The royals will head back to the White House for an elaborate state dinner this evening.

↓↓44UAE’s Saudi schism deepens with move to quit OPEC

 Matthew Martin
  
Maxim Shemetov/File Photo/Reuters 
Maxim Shemetov/File Photo/ReutersThe UAE’s announcement that it will quit the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries is a [blow to the cartel](https://semafor.com/s/fUfCjLkm5o?email=wschenk@gmail.com), which has struggled in recent years to maintain unity. In the short term, the announcement doesn’t really matter. With the Strait of Hormuz closed, Gulf oil producers can’t hit their production targets anyway. But Abu Dhabi has long complained that OPEC unfairly held back its oil production, and longer-term, the logic of maxing out crude sales when you can has increased. A deeper fault line is at play, too. The UAE’s move is the latest sign that it is no longer willing to go along with historic alliances it views as unnecessary purely for the sake of harmony. That most notably impacts its relations with Saudi Arabia, which considers itself the leader of the Gulf countries and bristles at the UAE’s independence.

↓↓55‘Chinamaxxing’ isn’t enough

 Andy Browne
  
Tyrone Siu/Reuters 
Tyrone Siu/ReutersAfter a decade of animosity, opinion polls show Americans’ views of China are softening, especially among the young. Online, Gen Z influencers are [“Chinamaxxing”](https://semafor.com/s/pSdDsyU2Jj?email=wschenk@gmail.com) — posting video clips of themselves sipping hot water, shuffling around in bedroom slippers, and eating hotpot, habits they encountered after last year’s ban on TikTok drove them to Chinese apps. A budding fascination with all things Chinese is boosting US tourist travel to China, albeit from a low base. Likewise, scholarly exchanges are picking up. And American business leaders are trickling back to China: Executives from about 30 US companies, led by Apple’s outgoing CEO, Tim Cook, showed up at the recent [China Development Forum in Beijing](https://semafor.com/s/LSHoCpmM6B?email=wschenk@gmail.com). But a combination of catchy memes, holiday excursions, and earnest seminars don’t add up to a broad détente between the US and China. If anything, they highlight the gulf that now separates them.
