> Colossus
> By Ross Barkan
> Arcade Publishing, 288 pages, $29.99

The American dream, according to Slavoj Žižek, amounts to “dematerialization” of real life as it becomes a “spectral show.” In this regard, claims Žižek, the “fake purity” of the fundamentalist preachers who “turned out to be secret sexual perverts,” is less a sign of their hypocrisy than of their consistency—their sticking to the artificiality of American life. 

This American attempt to transcend the limitations of the real, dressed in language of culture war and religious fervor, is at the heart of Ross Barkan’s fourth novel, Colossus. Its protagonist is Teddy Starr, the pastor of an evangelical Protestant church in a small town in the Midwest. Pastor Starr harps on the evils of the godless left, with its sexual libertinism and cultural Marxism, while several proverbial skeletons hang in his closet. Predictably, these include adulterous affairs with several members of his flock—which he has managed to morally justify.

But the greater secret he’s hiding from his congregation is the fact that he grew up in a Hasidic Jewish enclave in Brooklyn. He has justified this lie by convincing himself that he is not a Jew, and never really was. The life he has built for himself, from his zealous Christian faith to his new non-Jewish name, is the real Teddy, so much so that his past as Reuvain no longer exists.

But the past finds a way to catch up with him. Take his highly lucrative real estate endeavors which, as much as he tries to deny it, reveal the influence of his father, Yitzhak Gant, an infamous slumlord who owns nineteen buildings with 316 units. Yitzhak tells his son time and time again to “own,” and not to rent. His determination to own land is motivated by both practical and religious reasons: “Land, next to God, was the greatest currency.” Yitzhak incorporates the trope of a “promised land” after a history of persecution and exile into his business model. In his eyes, tenants are, “next to apostate Jews, the very worst kind of people.” He warns his son to avoid this fate “like a pharaoh berating his son about not becoming a slave.”

Despite his repudiation of his upbringing, Teddy reworks his father’s logic into his newfound theological framework. God may be the creator, “but you are in His shadow, imbued with his universe-shaping wisdom and grace.” Our words and actions, he reflects, “begin as a bridge to reality and become, with enough effort and execution, reality itself.” “Building,” he goes on, “is what we do, as people. It’s what God wants of us. To strive—to exist as gloriously as we can, in His image.” On top of fulfilling God’s will, land ownership is a matter of being practical: “Land appreciates. Recessions, depressions, and pandemics can’t undo land.” 

While Teddy tries to be patient and understanding as a landlord, doing everything he can to avoid having to evict his tenants, he insists that their not being in a position to own is a result of their own poor choices. The real “secret” to life is “to believe you’ve made it…You tell yourself, right now, that you’ve made it. You affirm it. You act, and you create that reality.” He insists that unlike “manifesting”—which is “woo-woo” and “the devil’s work”—this is how “God sees you.” He applies the same growth mindset to his work as a pastor at Pine Haven church, where by his own request he is paid on commission, with his rate increasing if the church grows. 

> “What is absent from Teddy’s theology is the recognition of man’s contingency.”

What is absent from Teddy’s theology is the recognition of man’s contingency and his dependence on the divine. Man is called to obey God’s will—which is fulfilled not in individual success, but in communion with him. In Teddy’s religion of self-creation, God becomes an appendage to the successful individual.

But self-creation only goes so far. Teddy’s brother tracks him down and  admonishes him, “there is nothing to replace blood…A name change, a wife, children, property—do you think it’s really anything against the past?” It turns out that even a self-made man owes something to others.

Barkan is a master of building suspense. His previous novel [https://www.compactmag.com/r/a2f6909a?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] had me turning page after page to get to the climax, but Colossus gripped my attention even more—to the point that I couldn’t help but continue reading as I walked from the subway onto the street, deterred neither by the raindrops ruining the book’s pages nor the pedestrians glaring at me. Indeed, there’s something about reading a Ross Barkan novel while riding the subway—or walking the streets—in New York that adds to the effect of the plot. In a globalized, increasingly artificial world where affection for place is no longer in fashion—and in some cases is deemed deplorable—his love for the city is refreshingly real.
