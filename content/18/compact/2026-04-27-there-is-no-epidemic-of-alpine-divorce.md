Guilhem Vellut

 

 

On April 9, a doctor in Hawaii was found guilty of trying to kill his wife while out on a seemingly romantic mountain hike. In February, an Austrian man was convicted of negligent manslaughter for leaving his girlfriend on the side of the Grossglockner mountain, where she died. Women online have responded to these news items by sharing their own stories of being abandoned by their boyfriends on hiking trails. And so a new iteration of “toxic masculinity” has caught the algorithm, with videos marked “#AlpineDivorce” racking up hundreds of thousands of views and features in The Guardian [https://www.compactmag.com/r/fde9c69e?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] and The New York Times [https://www.compactmag.com/r/0e2a3233?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] covering the phenomenon.

As with many internet coinages, “Alpine Divorce” has been applied to a wide range of otherwise unrelated behavior: Turning an argument into a breakup while on a long walk; getting too far ahead of a less experienced or fit romantic partner on a trail; making the wrong call in an emergency situation on a high-stakes mountaineering feat; and, well, murdering your significant other in the wilderness. 

The term is lifted from an 1896 short story [https://www.compactmag.com/r/3143084d?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] by Robert Barr about a husband’s plot to murder his wife in the Swiss Alps; the twist is that he realizes that she has already planned to commit suicide and frame him for her murder. The sinister undertones have stuck to the term, even if the details of the plot haven’t. 

On the surface, outdoor activities make for an optimal dating scene. Great views, a bit of risk and adrenaline, even getting out of a minor scrape together, are all great for romance. Outdoor dating has its own etiquette. One prominent climbing site ran a piece [https://www.compactmag.com/r/c66d651d?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] on “how to turn your Belaytionship into a Relationship”—an excellent climbing pun. Even outside the world of bouldering hobbyists, wilderness excursions have become a popular date idea. 

But the threat of the Alpine Divorce has turned the romantic getaway sour. Instagram has already primed Gen Z to think of their dating lives in pathological terms. A plethora of pop psychology accounts train you to spot manipulative, controlling, or abusive tendencies in potential partners. The same theories can be applied to outdoor dating: One article [https://www.compactmag.com/r/e36fabea?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] helps readers spot “Red Flags in a Belaytionship.” The Austrian case has been turned into an advice column [https://www.compactmag.com/r/805ebc4e?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] on “empowering yourself against an Alpine Divorce.” Bad dates become part of a larger pathological pattern of toxic masculinity, suppressed emotions, and runaway male ego.

Shorn of TikTok-therapy speak, the advice of those warning about the Alpine Divorce is sound. Don’t go on hikes beyond your level of fitness or expertise. Bring the right gear. Do your own research on the trail, even if you are going with a more experienced hiker. But there are downsides to applying the logic of criminal cases to your love life. 

What exactly happened [https://www.compactmag.com/r/73db57ef?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] on Grossglockner is still unclear, and Thomas P. (his full name has been withheld due to Austrian privacy laws) has appealed his conviction. The mistakes detailed in the conviction were relatively common [https://www.compactmag.com/r/4dc72911?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637] ones among amateur hikers: overestimating your abilities, misjudging the weather conditions, carrying the wrong gear, failing to call for help in time. This tragedy may have had nothing to do with “Alpine Divorce;” it might well have been a case of two strong-willed people in over their heads. The great outdoors attracts a certain type, and for those who attempt to scale impressive peaks, risk and danger are part of the thrill. The case should raise questions about amateur climbers, risk, and responsibility—not about toxic masculinity. 

The other reports of Alpine Divorce which followed the conviction start looking far more mundane the more you look into them. In one viral video [https://www.compactmag.com/r/8122c6c4?m=33b1996b-f427-4fb3-88a9-83a2095d7637], a woman is seen crying and shakily finishing a hike on her own after being abandoned by an inconsiderate partner. But she passed multiple other people following the same trail. Far from being stranded on a desolate hillside, she had only to follow a well-populated trail back to civilization. The scorned lover was not put in physical danger by a maliciously inconsiderate boyfriend—though that relationship probably wasn’t worth pursuing further.

Often, the Alpine Divorce narrative simply pathologizes ordinary inattentiveness and risk-taking. In many cases, what is happening is simply a case of mismatched expectations: men are more likely to treat a hike as a strenuous feat, something to be completed and mastered, while women are more likely to treat it as a leisurely stroll. Some men might be unable to cope with slow walking, or mistakenly think that running ahead will impress their date, but women—now often raised to think that they can do anything a man can do—can let their self-image get in the way of admitting defeat. In a culture that downplays sex differences, women refuse to ask for help, and men feel awkward being chivalrous. 

> “Gen Z is pessimistic enough about dating already.”

Gen Z is pessimistic enough about dating already. There is no epidemic of boyfriends and husbands leaving their partners to die in the wilderness, and extrapolating from these cases only makes already anxious young women less likely to leave their bedrooms. There’s minimal risk in going on the third date with that guy from Hinge who took the initiative to plan something as romantic as a hike. Just be sure to pack enough water to get home by yourself.
