A Marxist assassination thriller set in alternate-future Wales
The Volunteers , Raymond Williams 
The Welsh Marxist Raymond Williams is best-known for his influential works of history and literary criticism, but he was also a prolific novelist, often writing about politics and generational change in Wales (sometimes multi-multi-generational: His final, unfinished project was a saga tracking ordinary people in a town in Wales dating back to the Paleolithic). But he also wrote a fine political thriller: The Volunteers, an account of an ex-radical named Lewis Redfern, now employed by a sort of private journalism agency, investigating the attempted assassination of a politician during a period of labor unrest in Wales. Taking as his start the recent killing of a worker during a labor action, Redfern methodically uncovers a conspiracy that potentially implicates not just establishment politicians but his own firm. Published in 1978 but set in what was at the time the near future of the 1980s (and is now the middle-distant past), The Volunteers works quite well as a conspiracy thriller--filled with opaque dialog, oblique plotting, and a semi-cynical protagonist--and a fascinating historical document/failed prophecy, but it also has a depth and literary intelligence that can be hard to find in books of its ilk. There’s a scene involving the police re-running the assassination attempt, fitting into a larger motif of doubling and twinning threaded throughout the book, that I will think about for a while.

Eli Miller on Zohran Mamdani’s no-win budget choices :
Raiding the reserves would be worse, because they are supposed to be for acute periods of economic crisis, like 9/11, the 2008 financial crisis, or the COVID-19 pandemic. While I will admit that the vibes are not amazing at the moment, and there have been some ominous economic indicators  recently, we are still absolutely not in one of those acute periods. The local economy has been booming over the past few years, and FY2025 saw an 8% year-over-year increase in tax revenue . The reserves are often called the city’s “rainy day fund,” and it is not raining.
The substantive analysis hardly matters, anyway, because both of these ideas are functionally impossible. Julie Menin, Donovan Richards, Mark Levine, and basically every single other city elected official immediately objected  to the property tax increase. Mike Konczal on seeking explanations for the “vibecession” :
Imagine a person near the bottom of the income distribution, who is spending as much or more than they make as a result of borrowing. This makes them very sensitive to specific costs, like food, electricity, and auto insurance, all of which are hard to cut.2  Even if essentials rose in proportion, the risks of having little should make people concerned about absolute prices. But these items rose much faster than overall prices, as shown in Figure 1 above. For those with low incomes, the squeeze here is particularly difficult, and if you view utility as spending above a certain baseline , that baseline increasing faster causes distress. […]
The median sale price of a newly built home bought with an FHA mortgage, a common financing path for first-time buyers, went from about  $240,000 in 2019 to around $356,000 in late 2025, up roughly 48%. The 30-year mortgage rate went from  around 4% in 2019 to over 6% now, which means the same house costs more every month even before the sticker price moves. Homeowners benefit from the wealth, but they also realize any new home they are going to buy has gone up in price too, and interest rates are higher. Fully-anticipated inflation can make home-buying more inaccessible (Modigliani and Lessard, 1975) . But given the even higher costs here, it is easy to see how this causes angst.
I think the absolute level also matters for tail risk situations, where sudden, unexpected costs could impose a much higher penalty even adjusted for inflation. Let’s take health care. Kaiser Family Foundation’s  Employer Health Benefits Survey reports that family coverage premiums in 2024 are up 24% since 2019, comparable to overall inflation. But the average isn’t what you worry about, it’s the tail. In any given year you might face a plan that dropped your doctor this cycle, a partial denial on a procedure, or a specialty referral that sends you outside the network. And now the premium tax credits have expired, while there’s also a trillion dollars of cuts to Medicaid on the horizon.
Natalie Wolchover on the bacterial flagellar motor :
Since the discovery of the bacterial flagellar motor in the 1970s, biologists and creationists alike have marveled at its design like medieval architects staring with awe at the dome of the Pantheon built by their Roman ancestors. It’s hard to fathom the level of engineering achievable by a billion years of bacterial evolution, especially with only 20 minutes between cell generations, which allows for a truly astronomical number of mutations and trial runs. Creationists hold up the bacterial flagellar motor as a prime example of intelligent design — specifically the concept of “irreducible complexity,” a biological system so intricate, they say, that it couldn’t possibly have arisen in stages through the gradual, stepwise process of Darwinian evolution.
Yet it very much did. 
Over the past few decades, scientists have toiled to unravel how the flagellar motor works — namely, how it rotates and switches directions.
Now they finally have. A wave of studies since 2020 has cracked the molecular structures of the flagellar motor’s parts, including, most importantly, the small cogwheels that turn the larger cogwheel at the flagellum’s base. The final pieces of this dynamic puzzle fell into place as recently as March 2026.
R. Emmet Sweeney on Jason Statham’s late period :
With the formation of Punch Palace Productions and a re-focusing on his core talents, Statham is on the path to reclaim the mantle of Gen X Charles Bronson, a working class granite-jawed stoic who excels at excessive violence. Bronson grew up in a mining family, while Statham’s father taught him how to sell dodgy jewelry on street corners, which is where modeling agencies and Guy Ritchie plucked him from shirtless obscurity and into shirtless stardom. The Jason Statham promise, as with peak Bronson, is that of a hard-nosed worker who provides factory-like output and reliability, doing exactly what it says on the tin. As he continues to tweak the blueprints for his action machinery, the nearly 60-year-old Statham is on track to make his golden years another golden age.
A rock-’em-sock-’em French car chase and martial-arts action movie
Lost Bullet (2020), dir. Guillaume Pierret
Streaming on Netflix
I complain a lot about the action-movie slop Netflix generates with various Hemsworth and Russo brothers, but credit where credit is due: Lost Bullet, a French flick about a criminal mechanic on the run from the law trying to clear his name, rocks. There is very little of the grey, goopy, fix-it-in-post-style slurry that characterizes most American Netflix action movies; instead what you get is some really good physical filmmaking, complete with great loud car chases and some tremendously weighty and painful-looking martial arts sequences. The plot is whatever; I can barely remember which guys are which; but the actors are great, especially Alban Lenoir as the main guy, Lino. I don’t know how I’ve missed them, but there are actually three of these; I’m extremely excited to check out the next two in the series.
