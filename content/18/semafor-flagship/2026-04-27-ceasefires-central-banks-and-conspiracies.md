↓↓11Israel-Lebanon truce frays

 
Shir Torem/Reuters 
Shir Torem/ReutersIsraeli strikes on Lebanon killed at least 14 people, pushing an already shaky ceasefire close to collapse. Israel and Lebanon [agreed a three-week truce extension this month](https://semafor.com/s/Znxcq2Yqjn?email=wschenk@gmail.com), but Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said Israeli troops were “acting with force” in Lebanon to respond to Hezbollah-launched drones and missiles; Netanyahu accused Hezbollah of “dismantling the ceasefire.” [Israeli attacks have killed at least 36 since the US-mediated truce began](https://semafor.com/s/c5VfrIZk2P?email=wschenk@gmail.com), AFP estimated, and Beirut says more than 2,500 have died in total. The UN said that more than [1 million people have been displaced](https://semafor.com/s/eud7jXmLx9?email=wschenk@gmail.com) — almost a fifth of the country’s population — with 200,000 fleeing into neighboring Syria.

↓↓22Hopes fade for US-Iran talks

 
Iran reportedly offered to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, but insisted US nuclear talks be postponed, a condition Washington is unlikely to accept. The stalled negotiations are leading to fears for the global economy: [Oil rose and S&P futures](https://semafor.com/s/9PqTOqY5iq?email=wschenk@gmail.com) fell on signs that Washington and Tehran were no closer to a lasting truce after US President Donald Trump [canceled American participation](https://semafor.com/s/jjzg8dbARX?email=wschenk@gmail.com) in talks with Iran. Though Trump has insisted Washington has the upper hand against Tehran, former US officials warned that he would have to be [patient, diplomatic, and willing to make concessions](https://semafor.com/s/3FdGkCUBWp?email=wschenk@gmail.com) in order to reach a durable deal. “Trump wants them to really just capitulate,” an Obama- and Biden-era negotiator told Bloomberg. “[That’s never going to happen](https://semafor.com/s/I1tiPtZpXC?email=wschenk@gmail.com).”

↓↓33Central banks set to hold fire

 
Major global central banks are set to hold interest rates steady this week, but are expected to diverge on monetary policy once the fallout from the Middle East conflict becomes clearer. Traders are betting that the Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, Bank of Japan, and Bank of England will all [maintain rates at current levels](https://semafor.com/s/itSneRoW1c?email=wschenk@gmail.com). The [ECB](https://semafor.com/s/6p7wB4IVNf?email=wschenk@gmail.com) and [BOJ](https://semafor.com/s/CgaHBRLGCH?email=wschenk@gmail.com) are seen as more hawkish, however, with hikes due later in the year or in 2027, whereas markets are pricing in [a 35% chance of a Fed rate cut by year-end](https://semafor.com/s/IBH0sz28uN?email=wschenk@gmail.com). This week’s patience offers some respite for developing nations, which often borrow in dollars or euros to court foreign investors, leaving them vulnerable to richer countries tightening monetary policy.

↓↓44Cairo’s nightlife returns

 
Egypt lifted early-closure measures on Cairo after a month of the famously late-night city being forced to shut down by 11pm. Cairenes are used to sitting at cafes until the small hours, but energy price spikes from the Iran war pushed authorities to impose a curfew, and dim or shut off streetlights, one of several such efforts to preserve fuel worldwide. It hit the capital’s economy badly — “[All of [Cairo residents’] work is done at the coffee shop](https://semafor.com/s/j1e939kfM6?email=wschenk@gmail.com),” one owner told Reuters — and led to [a revival of COVID-era speakeasy tactics](https://semafor.com/s/857iRx9HUv?email=wschenk@gmail.com), with ’ahwas closing shutters and keeping an eye out for police. [Residents are welcoming the return](https://semafor.com/s/2onJm9z01H?email=wschenk@gmail.com): “The streets were really depressing for the past month,” one told Bloomberg.

↓↓66UK hopes king’s US visit builds ties

 
Ian Vogler/Pool via Reuters 
Ian Vogler/Pool via ReutersKing Charles III arrives in Washington today with Britain hoping the royal touch can help ease increasingly strained relations with the US. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer has infuriated US President Donald Trump by refusing to take part in the Iran war; in theory the state visit is unrelated, but Downing Street hopes [it might lower the temperature](https://semafor.com/s/7WTcH9ees9?email=wschenk@gmail.com). Péter Magyar. Bernadett Szabo/File Photo/Reuters

↓↓99Latam’s worsening demographics

 
Latin America has become the fastest-aging part of the world, raising fears that it will grow old before it can become rich. For decades, the region had one of the world’s largest demographic bonuses, with births per woman [peaking at almost six in the 1960s](https://semafor.com/s/HhHrQRoEl2?email=wschenk@gmail.com). However population growth has since plummeted to well below replacement levels: Chile, one of Latin America’s wealthiest countries, now has a fertility rate below that of Japan, the global archetype of a demographic crisis. The crisis will shape “politics, businesses, communities, [and how people live for decades to come,](https://semafor.com/s/n1plNsdBrf?email=wschenk@gmail.com)” Americas Quarterly said.

↓↓1010US happiness plummets

 
Tyrone Siu/Reuters 
Tyrone Siu/ReutersThe US has become startlingly less happy despite being, by most metrics, significantly richer, thanks in large part to interlinked post-pandemic aftershocks, a leading political writer argued. Americans’ reported well-being was declining steadily for years, but since 2020 has dropped swiftly across all demographics. The suddenness and uniformity imply the usual suspects — smartphones, societal atomization — [cannot be the whole story](https://semafor.com/s/xQfWjRbYx7?email=wschenk@gmail.com), Derek Thompson said. Instead he suggested high inflation has created a “vibecession,” visible in other Western countries, while collapsing trust in institutions and a series of crises filtered through a negative news environment have further eroded public sentiment. The US “was a reasonably happy country for a long time,” Thompson quoted an economist saying. “It is not happy now.”

But “for the first time in years, outright victory seems possible.”…And Justice for AllModern political philosophy is dominated by one man: John Rawls. Of the field’s five most-cited 20th-century works, two are by Rawls, and three are responses to him. “Every decade or so a new book comes along, promising to shift the paradigm,” says the political philosopher Joseph Heath in Persuasion. “Each one has fizzled out, sending us all back to Rawls.” Before Rawls, philosophers largely tried to answer first-order questions about what it meant to create a good society. Rawls, instead, treated it as a political problem: Given that we have many groups who disagree about what a just world looks like, what principles do we need to protect our abilities to pursue those ideals?Some modern conservative thinkers have proposed a post-liberal vision, but, says Heath, they are in an important sense pre-Rawls; they make no effort to think politically about justice. They “are still playing the old game, seemingly oblivious to how Rawls changed the terms of the discussion.” Rawls would have responded, Heath says, along the lines of: Well done, [you’ve expressed your own conception of the good life](https://semafor.com/s/guqvxMFcEB?email=wschenk@gmail.com); now you just need to convince everyone else to follow it.
