# Week 03 Books Newsletter Analysis (January 12-17, 2026)

## Overview

This week's books newsletters featured an extraordinary deep-dive into problem-solving methodology and intelligence, extensive coverage of new fiction releases including several debut novels, and significant attention to international literature in translation. The intersection of literature with current geopolitics (particularly Iran and Venezuela) was notable, as was ongoing discourse about the publishing industry's mechanics and literary anniversaries.

## Surprising Insights

- **"Some people are just smarter than others"** - [Mr. and Mrs. Psmith's Bookshelf](https://open.substack.com/pub/thepsmiths/p/review-how-to-solve-it-by-george?utm_source=email&redirect=app-store&utm_campaign=email-read-in-app) boldly engages with the taboo topic of innate intelligence differences while arguing that intelligence can still be systematically improved through methodological problem-solving.

- **"I essentially stalked Jackson to a party in a warehouse in Chinatown"** - [Book Gossip](https://link.nymag.com/view/6356a99e6d2ff57c1382dd28pwql1.12ut/d3bb1ab2) reveals debut novelist Madeline Cash's unconventional path to publication, where she pursued her eventual FSG editor at a party rather than going through traditional submission channels.

- **"This is not an instruction manual for a debut writer... it's probably how people get restraining orders"** - [Book Gossip](https://link.nymag.com/view/6356a99e6d2ff57c1382dd28pwql1.12ut/d3bb1ab2) offers refreshingly honest self-awareness about the non-replicable nature of unconventional publishing success.

- **"Hocquard distinguishes between classic and inverse elegiac poets"** - [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-aejyo-36F2A313-iruduywlh-a) introduces an intriguing taxonomy: classic elegiac poets ruminate on the past while inverse elegiac poets remake it.

- **"I had splendid Chinese food at Chef Yan in Fairfax in a cluster of shops I would never have thought to look in"** - [Henry Oliver / The Common Reader](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/driving-like-a-video-game) provides cultural observation from a British perspective on American suburban geography and the surprising convenience of car-centric life despite alarming driving behaviors.

- **"Many hundreds or even thousands of protesters have already been killed"** - [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-afllo-36F2A313-iruduywlh-z) reports on the devastating human cost of Iranian government crackdowns during internet blackouts.

- **Key Points**:
  - Extensive review of George Polya's *How to Solve It* as a methodology for boosting "effective intelligence"
  - Argues intelligence should be defined as "the ability of an agent to achieve its goals"
  - Covers Polya's four stages of problem-solving: understanding, planning, executing, reflecting

- **Unique Angle**: Combines philosophical inquiry about intelligence with practical problem-solving methodology, then extends to speculations about AI capabilities and why ancient civilizations didn't advance faster.

## Patterns and Trends

**1. The Publishing Industry's Hidden Mechanics**: [Book Gossip](https://link.nymag.com/view/6356a99e6d2ff57c1382dd28pwql1.12ut/d3bb1ab2)'s interview with Madeline Cash reveals a publishing ecosystem where personal networking, self-promotion, and unconventional approaches can circumvent traditional gatekeeping - though as Cash herself notes, this is "probably how people get restraining orders" rather than a replicable model.

**2. Intelligence and AI as Literary Concerns**: The [Mr. and Mrs. Psmith's Bookshelf](https://open.substack.com/pub/thepsmiths/p/review-how-to-solve-it-by-george?utm_source=email&redirect=app-store&utm_campaign=email-read-in-app) review demonstrates how books-focused newsletters are increasingly engaging with AI implications, framing questions about machine intelligence through classical problem-solving methodology.

**3. Geopolitics Through Literary Lenses**: Both the Iran and Venezuela coverage from [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-afllo-36F2A313-iruduywlh-z) show literary publications serving as venues for serious political analysis, often providing more nuanced and historically-grounded perspectives than mainstream news.

**4. Translation as Cultural Bridge**: [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-aejyo-36F2A313-iruduywlh-a) and [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-aetrz-36F2A313-iruduywlh-jh) consistently champion translated and international literature, positioning themselves against an English-language parochialism in the book world.

**5. Canon Expansion**: [Henry Oliver](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/bacon-swift-smith-three-anniversaries) makes a case for expanding the literary canon to include "rational, discursive, and argumentative" works like essays and treatises, challenging the novel's dominance in how we think about literature.

**6. Commercial vs. Literary Divide**: The contrast between [Bookshop.org](https://ctrk.klclick.com/l/01KEVT7H4P417X1ZMAYEKB3W4J_3)'s promotional emails and [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-aejyo-36F2A313-iruduywlh-a)'s literary curation highlights the persistent divide between commercial bookselling and literary publishing, even as both serve readers.

**7. The Bible as Literature**: Marilynne Robinson's upcoming seminars, announced by [New York Review Books](https://thenewyorkreview.updatemyprofile.com/y-astujd-36F2A313-iruduywlh-p), suggest renewed interest in approaching religious texts through literary rather than devotional frameworks.
