# Week 03 Uncategorized Newsletter Analysis (January 13-17, 2026)

## Overview

This week's uncategorized newsletters span a remarkably diverse range of topics: AI-driven software development methodologies, geopolitical commentary on U.S.-Venezuela relations, critiques of tech industry wealth concentration, protocol governance in decentralized social networks, and the economics of Chinese AI labs. [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/10ca4a06?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1) dominated the technical discourse with multiple posts on autonomous coding loops, while [Everything is Political](http://r.mail.slowfactory.foundation/mk/cl/f/sh/1t6Af4OiGsEagEGigmVzxR1A2UYB62/Gzp2pmAfUDdU) and [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/who-stole-your-cookie/) offered sharp social and political commentary.

## Major Topics and Stories

### 1. AI-Driven Software Development: The "Ralph Loop" Methodology
**Coverage:** [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/a0e555fe?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1), [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/10ca4a06?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1), [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/fb27d3d3?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1)

Geoffrey Huntley continued his prolific output on autonomous AI-driven software development. His "Ralph Wiggum Technique" describes a methodology where AI agents develop software in loops at costs he claims are "less than a fast food worker's wage" ($10.42/hr). The approach fundamentally shifts how software is built: from vertical "Jenga brick" construction to iterative loops where code is "clay on the pottery wheel."

> "Software development is dead - I killed it. Software can now be developed cheaper than the wage of a burger flipper at maccas and it can be built autonomously whilst you are AFK." — [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/10ca4a06?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1)

In a related post on [back pressure](https://ghuntley.com/r/fb27d3d3?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1), Huntley discusses how to tune feedback mechanisms in AI coding loops, noting that pre-commit hooks, once annoying for human developers, become essential when "humans aren't the ones doing the software development."

Most surprisingly, Huntley revealed he has become "a walking, talking financial instrument" on the Solana cryptocurrency network, with speculators trading a $RALPH token based on his perceived ability to advance AI development. He claims this generated $300,000 in his bank account within a week, which he sees as potentially funding "truly independent research."

### 2. U.S. Intervention in Venezuela
**Coverage:** [Everything is Political](http://r.mail.slowfactory.foundation/mk/cl/f/sh/1t6Af4OiGsEagEGigmVzxR1A2UYB62/Gzp2pmAfUDdU), [Everything is Political](http://r.mail.slowfactory.foundation/mk/cl/f/sh/1t6Af4OiGsEagEGigmVzxR1A2UYB62/yXAMJ_DIXNnQ)

The Everything is Political newsletter dedicated two issues to U.S. military actions in Venezuela, framing them within broader patterns of American interventionism. The January 13 issue contextualized Trump's abduction of Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro as consistent with historical U.S. foreign policy rather than unprecedented, while the January 15 issue argued that "Sanctions & Embargoes only Hurt the People."

The coverage included multiple perspectives:
- "Trump's attack on Venezuela: An Exemplary Punishment" by Simon Rodriguez
- "Venezuela: Beyond False Choices" by Caribe Lunar
- "Dissonant Solidarity: Venezuela and the International Left" by Luken Ignacio Quintana

### 3. Tech Industry Wealth Inequality and Corporate Accountability
**Coverage:** [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/who-stole-your-cookie/), [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/when-the-tech-leopard-eats-your-face/)

Chris Ferdinandi's Go Make Things newsletter delivered pointed critiques of wealth concentration and tech industry dynamics. In "Who Stole Your Cookie?", he invoked the Pixar film A Bug's Life to argue that power systems pit disadvantaged groups against each other rather than addressing structural inequalities.

> "Billionaires should not exist. No one should have that much money. Not while people starve and die of exposure and preventable medical issues." — [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/who-stole-your-cookie/)

His follow-up piece on Tailwind CSS layoffs connected multiple threads: Adam Wathan firing 75% of his team due to AI's impact on documentation traffic, the irony of Tailwind having "devalued the skills and expertise of CSS professionals," and the subsequent sponsorship from Vercel and Google AI. Ferdinandi characterized the outcome as "a shitty company run funded by fascist money."

### 4. DeepSeek's "No Business Model" Advantage
**Coverage:** [Kevin Xu @ Interconnected](https://interconnected.blog/r/5907b444?m=13ee6092-18b4-4710-96ca-c96c6fd588dd)

Kevin Xu offered a counterintuitive analysis of DeepSeek's competitive position approaching the first anniversary of its breakthrough "DeepSeek Moment." While the Chinese AI lab's models are no longer the most capable, cheapest, or most open, Xu argues its enduring advantage is having no business model at all.

> "The road to AGI requires compute, talent, and more than a healthy dose of good research taste. No one said it requires a business model." — [Kevin Xu @ Interconnected](https://interconnected.blog/r/5907b444?m=13ee6092-18b4-4710-96ca-c96c6fd588dd)

Liang Wenfeng's self-funding from his quant fund High Flyer Capital (which earned $700 million profit on 53% returns in 2025) creates organizational benefits: no internal resource competition, no bureaucratic infighting over GPU allocation, and no jealousy over compensation. Xu contrasts this with heavily-funded labs that have "branded weight plates" at office gyms, noting that xAI just raised $20 billion despite Musk being one of the world's richest people.

### 5. ActivityPub Protocol Governance
**Coverage:** [Connected Places by Laurens Hof](https://connectedplaces.online/wp-admin/admin-ajax.php?action=tnptr&nltr=ODI7NjU4O2h0dHBzOi8vY29ubmVjdGVkcGxhY2VzLm9ubGluZS93cC1hZG1pbi9hZG1pbi1hamF4LnBocD9hY3Rpb249dG5wJm5hPXYmbms9NjU4LTNmNmU2NDBmZjImaWQ9ODI7O2Y5MjVkZDBhZThjMWNjMzI2YzM0MDEyYWY5ZTJiNTIy)

Laurens Hof analyzed the W3C's announcement of a new Social Web Working Group to maintain ActivityPub, revealing a governance paradox at the heart of the fediverse's decentralization claims.

> "This is by all accounts an extremely funny outcome for a network that aims to be independent of Big Tech's power." — [Connected Places by Laurens Hof](https://connectedplaces.online/wp-admin/admin-ajax.php?action=tnptr&nltr=ODI7NjU4O2h0dHBzOi8vY29ubmVjdGVkcGxhY2VzLm9ubGluZS93cC1hZG1pbi9hZG1pbi1hamF4LnBocD9hY3Rpb249dG5wJm5hPXYmbms9NjU4LTNmNmU2NDBmZjImaWQ9ODI7O2Y5MjVkZDBhZThjMWNjMzI2YzM0MDEyYWY5ZTJiNTIy)

The analysis reveals that only two fediverse-active organizations are paid W3C members: Meta and the Social Web Foundation (which receives Meta funding). Mastodon, Framasoft, and other open-source fediverse builders cannot participate unless invited. Meanwhile, Mastodon's implementation choices become de facto standards regardless of whether it engages with formal governance, creating a "vacuum where the largest implementer is able to set standards for the rest of the network, but without the governance or formal documentation."

### 6. Go Language Updates and Technical Content
**Coverage:** [Golang Weekly](https://golangweekly.com/issues/585)

The Golang Weekly newsletter covered Go 1.26 RC2's release with security fixes, alongside practical technical content including debates about atomic operations versus mutexes, implementing mutexes from scratch, and struct pointer semantics. A notable highlight: a Go runtime for the Sega Dreamcast (libgodc), and the observation that "even Linus Torvalds is 'vibe coding' now" using Google's Antigravity tool.

## Cross-Newsletter Patterns

### AI's Disruptive Economic Impact
Both [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/10ca4a06?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1) and [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/when-the-tech-leopard-eats-your-face/) addressed AI's impact on employment, though from very different angles. Huntley celebrates software development becoming cheaper than fast food wages, while Ferdinandi critiques how Tailwind's AI-driven documentation decline led to layoffs while executives secure corporate sponsorships.

### Governance and Power Structures
Multiple newsletters examined how power concentrates despite ostensibly open systems. [Connected Places](https://connectedplaces.online/wp-admin/admin-ajax.php?action=tnptr&nltr=ODI7NjU4O2h0dHBzOi8vY29ubmVjdGVkcGxhY2VzLm9ubGluZS93cC1hZG1pbi9hZG1pbi1hamF4LnBocD9hY3Rpb249dG5wJm5hPXYmbms9NjU4LTNmNmU2NDBmZjImaWQ9ODI7O2Y5MjVkZDBhZThjMWNjMzI2YzM0MDEyYWY5ZTJiNTIy) showed how W3C membership requirements give Meta more protocol governance power than actual fediverse builders. [Kevin Xu](https://interconnected.blog/r/5907b444?m=13ee6092-18b4-4710-96ca-c96c6fd588dd) argued that avoiding venture capital preserves organizational alignment, while [Go Make Things](https://gomakethings.com/who-stole-your-cookie/) framed billionaire philanthropy as PR that masks systemic wealth concentration.

### Alternative Funding Models
Both [Geoffrey Huntley](https://ghuntley.com/r/a0e555fe?m=6d763223-fea6-4f7b-98af-864085a0f8a1) and [Kevin Xu](https://interconnected.blog/r/5907b444?m=13ee6092-18b4-4710-96ca-c96c6fd588dd) explored funding models outside traditional venture capital. Huntley's Solana-based creator token and DeepSeek's quant-fund self-financing represent attempts to pursue ambitious technical goals without the commercialization pressure that comes with outside investment.
