# Week 05 Misc Newsletter Analysis (Jan 27-Feb 2, 2026)

## Overview

Week 05's miscellaneous newsletters present a striking convergence between AI's transformation of work and deeply human themes of meaning, suffering, and creativity. [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower) framed management skills as the new AI superpower, while [Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting) warned that AGI functions primarily as a marketing smokescreen obscuring real AI harms. [Ajeya Cotra](https://acotra.substack.com/p/the-ea-psyche) dissected the effective altruism mindset, [Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness) explored what makes machines truly alive, and [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera) chronicled a founder's obsessive quest to rescue a spacecraft 53 million kilometers from Earth. Against this backdrop of technological optimism and philosophical inquiry, [The Free Press](https://substack.com/redirect/44dbf5dc-05d0-47b8-be3b-460e0ca56ae9?j=eyJ1IjoiMWllZnkifQ.sG3OqnkHDNZpplC0eLA9u0NYhAE4J8yHwSPDyeVA8k8) covered political turmoil in Minneapolis, while [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering) explored why we cling to pain as identity. Fashion, gene editing, and strategic thinking rounded out a week that asked fundamental questions about what we value and who we're becoming.

## Major Topics and Stories

### 1. AI Transforms Work Through Management, Not Just Technology

**Coverage:** [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower), [Scott Werner](https://worksonmymachine.ai/p/the-different-shapes-of-think-before), [Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting)

The week's most compelling AI insight came not from model capabilities but from organizational dynamics. [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower) documented an experimental MBA class where students built working startups in four days using AI tools—achieving results "an order of magnitude further along" than typical semester-long projects. His key insight: "The skills that are so often dismissed as 'soft' turned out to be the hard ones."

> "I wish I could say this impressive output was the result of my brilliant teaching, but we don't really have a great framework yet for how to use all these tools, the students largely figured it out on their own. It helped that they had some management and subject matter expertise because it turns out that the key to success was actually the last bit of the previous paragraph: telling the AI what you want." — [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower)

Mollick introduced an equation for agentic work: whether to delegate to AI depends on three variables—Human Baseline Time (how long you'd take), Probability of Success (how likely AI will deliver), and AI Process Time (cost of requesting and evaluating). With GPT-5.2 tying or beating human experts 72% of the time in OpenAI's GDPval paper, the calculation increasingly favors AI delegation—but only when paired with clear instructions, effective feedback, and good evaluation skills.

[Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting), speaking to California's Legislative Progressive Caucus, offered a starkly different perspective. Drawing on two centuries of automation history, he argued that AGI claims serve primarily as cover for immediate labor extraction and regulatory evasion.

> "AGI, or an AI super intelligence should, in my opinion as a tech reporter and something of a historian of automation, be understood primarily as Emily Bender have described it: A marketing device." — [Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting)

Merchant documented a pattern stretching from 1800s mechanized looms to today: entrepreneurs use stories about revolutionary technology to override norms and laws. Factory owners convinced British Parliament to tear up cloth worker protections, leading to "decades of immense working class suffering." Today's AI CEOs argue for regulatory moratoriums using the same playbook. He tracked how OpenAI CEO Sam Altman's AGI rhetoric "tended to correlate almost exactly to when they needed to raise a new funding round or distract from a PR scandal."

The real-world impacts are immediate: companies executing AI-justified layoffs even when the technology can't actually do the work. A copy chief named Jacques told Merchant: "I was actually let go the week before Thanksgiving now that the AI was good enough." His job had been training AI to replace entry-level workers, eliminating the ladder for others to climb.

[Scott Werner](https://worksonmymachine.ai/p/the-different-shapes-of-think-before) complemented this with a whimsical but substantive exploration of "think before you build" prompting techniques through the allegory of a beaver consulting an Oracle. He outlined five approaches: Deductive (articulate principles first), Inductive (show examples, trust pattern recognition), Abductive (extract explicit principles from examples), Contrapositive (define failure modes to avoid), and DSL Scaffolding (build a language for the problem domain).

> "The Oracle knows how to build. But it doesn't always know how it knows. When you ask it to build and figure out quality at the same time, things get confused." — [Scott Werner](https://worksonmymachine.ai/p/the-different-shapes-of-think-before)

### 2. The Effective Altruism Psyche: Taking Ideas (Too?) Seriously

**Coverage:** [Ajeya Cotra](https://acotra.substack.com/p/the-ea-psyche)

[Ajeya Cotra](https://acotra.substack.com/p/the-ea-psyche), who works on AI extreme risks at METR and has been "a pretty hardcore EA for over 15 years," published a frank anthropological guide to what makes effective altruists tick. She distilled EA psychology to two core traits: taking ideas seriously and radical empathy.

Taking ideas seriously means "very unusually willing to change our lives around based on explicit logical arguments" and "unusually unattached to the intuitions we've picked up about what's 'normal.'" This leads EAs to Lifecycle Investing, stocking up on PPE in January 2020, using dating docs to analyze relationships, and trying genetically engineered bacteria that prevent cavities.

Radical empathy means striving not to care more about beings "just because they are closer to us in space or time, just because we share a country or race or species." EAs "strive not to care more about pandas than pigs," "not to care more about humans than non-human animals," and "not to care more about biological creatures than we do about digital minds."

> "These are all arguments that treat clothes, fundamentally, as a symbolic language. But there's another element at play when it comes to Slow Clothes specifically: Circa 2026, we have grown unprecedentedly tired and mistrustful of the World of Symbols and Signs. We want to engage, instead, with the World of Things in Themselves." — [Ajeya Cotra](https://acotra.substack.com/p/the-ea-psyche)

Cotra plotted these traits on a graph where the top-right corner represents maximum EA-ness, and noted "there's a region of the graph — 'aiming to directly benefit exotic distant beings while making decisions using intuitive learned heuristics' — that's impossible to reach." The structural problem: when you care about the Tegmark IV multiverse, trial-and-error learning becomes impossible because "feedback signals you have available to train on" may not correlate with "the ultimate unobservable goal."

Her warning: "I think most hardcore EAs do have these other little virtues" (carefulness, humility, judgment, taste), but "it's a high-variance way of being." Many hardcore EAs "found their way to EA after first having been hardcore about Christianity," and she'd bet "today's hardcore EAs would have been disproportionately likely to be hardcore Marxists during the Great Depression."

### 3. Engineering Liveness: What Makes Machines Truly Alive?

**Coverage:** [Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness)

[Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness) published a meditation on liveness through the lens of the "useless machine"—a device whose only function is turning itself off when turned on. Venkatesh Rao argued this represents "the simplest example I've been able to find of an entity that seems to exhibit a form of liveness."

The key distinction: normal machines solve human problems (thermostats maintaining temperature). A useless machine "exists primarily to assert control over its own destiny, not to solve problems for us. It does not live to serve." The defining property of liveness is capture resistance.

> "Liveness does not necessarily dance. Its defining quality is that it asserts itself stubbornly and quietly, resisting capture and harness. Like a mussel clinging to a piling, claiming its share of sunlight and marine nutrients. Or a useless box turning itself off and going back to sleep." — [Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness)

Rao connected this to Werner Herzog's famous monologue about jungle nature as "the harmony of overwhelming and collective murder." The question for "New Nature"—technological systems with laws as inviolable as nature's—is where the murder equivalent appears. Not in Battle Bots or military hardware but in "vicious competitiveness lurking in boring, routine, near-invisibility."

He reimagined Darwin's tangled bank passage with technological entities: "It is interesting to contemplate a technological tangled bank, clothed with many technologies of many kinds, with mile-markers weaving by old railroads, with various vehicles flitting about, and with fiber crawling through the damp earth."

The useless machine's insight extends to broader life philosophy: "You have no obligation to be interesting or useful to the world." Liveness means "choosing to continue the game. Until it is time not to." What comes after uselessness? "Whatever it does, it cannot devote itself wholly to that task. Liveness must pay itself first."

### 4. The Spacecraft That Wouldn't Die: A Founder's Obsession

**Coverage:** [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera), [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/the-present-and-future-of-gene-editing-jennifer-doudna-crispr)

[Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera) published an extraordinary narrative about Epic Aerospace founder Ignacio Belieres Montero's year-long quest to rescue Chimera GEO-1, a space tug now 53 million kilometers from Earth.

Montero, a Stanford dropout who built rocket engines in his Buenos Aires dorm room, designed Chimera GEO-1 to be "kind of unkillable" with "essentially two of everything and redundant wiring in every single freaking place." Launched February 27, 2025, the spacecraft immediately encountered catastrophic communication problems. Within hours, it was hurtling toward deep space at kilometers per second with marginal ability to receive commands.

> "I remember cursing at the tug just before a pass, looking at the midnight sky, as many of the guys on our team probably did, and repeating to myself and the tug, 'I'm going to bring you back. I don't fucking care if you want me to or not, but I'm going to bring you back.'" — Ignacio Montero, [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera)

The story chronicles Montero's frenzied global ground station tour: begging for help from Goonhilly in England (supporting Intuitive's lunar lander), driving to Germany's Effelsberg Observatory unannounced and sleeping in his car by the gate, and methodically debugging communication protocols while the spacecraft receded millions of kilometers. By September, 33 million kilometers out, they finally tested the propulsion system and confirmed it could fire.

Now at 53 million kilometers, Montero wants NASA or ESA deep space antenna access to command the engine burn and bring Chimera home over a year-long journey. But agencies resist supporting a commercial (non-science) mission. He's considering pivoting: "Maybe to do this I will have to take some sort of detour on our way back home and take pictures of another planet, or an asteroid... In other words, he might need to turn this into a science project."

[Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/the-present-and-future-of-gene-editing-jennifer-doudna-crispr) also featured Nobel Prize winner Jennifer Doudna on CRISPR's present and future. While therapies like Casgevy represent breakthroughs, Doudna acknowledged the technology "has, in many ways, not lived up to the hope and hype just yet"—therapies remain expensive and difficult to distribute. She's convinced major breakthroughs are imminent.

### 5. The Creator Economy's Hidden Costs

**Coverage:** [Olga](https://dataanalysis.substack.com/p/celebrating-300th-newsletter), [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring)

[Olga](https://dataanalysis.substack.com/p/celebrating-300th-newsletter) marked her 300th newsletter with brutal honesty about the realities of consistent creation. After five years of weekly publishing without missing a single issue, reaching 30K readers and 60K monthly views, she distilled ten hard-won lessons that challenge the "content creator" mythology.

> "Imagine writing on a deadline every week for 5 years without sick days, vacations, holidays, work deadlines, or anything else getting in the way. You have to really love what you do and truly believe in the mission you're on." — [Olga](https://dataanalysis.substack.com/p/celebrating-300th-newsletter)

Her most counterintuitive insights: "Quantity over quality doesn't work anymore" (with 45,000+ data writers on Substack, only high-value evergreen content sustains growth), newsletters build credibility but not instant leads (90% of her consulting came from referrals, not newsletter readers), and the audience changes predictably in waves (0-100 = inner circle, 100-1K = students, 1K-10K = builders, 10K+ = senior practitioners).

The operational burden surprised her: "90% of writing is research," and paid subscriptions bring "taxes, invoicing, refund requests, group subscriptions, and general support" that consume unexpected hours. Yet she advises going paid early: "After I went paid (and upgraded the content), open rates increased, the same posts received more reads, the newsletter was taken more seriously, and, most importantly, I took it more seriously."

### 6. Fashion's Authenticity Crisis: The "Slow Clothes" Paradox

**Coverage:** [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring), [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/the-lo-fi-mark-of-a-real-one), [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/cashless-is-swagless-wallets-beanies-hoodies-2026-footwear-trends-styling-swag), [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/hot-and-nerdy)

[Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring) tackled fashion's existential contradiction: brands that rejected trend cycles and artificial scarcity have become inarguably trendy. In a sweeping 6,000+ word essay, they documented how "Slow Clothes"—small independent makers focused on craft, natural fabrics, and anti-fashion principles—reached critical mass and now face the corrupting forces of their own success.

> "The contradiction here, of course, is that these putatively anti-fashion labels, while remaining small, have now become inarguably fashionable: The 'anti-trend' ethos is, for the time being, extremely on trend." — [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring)

The piece opens with Minneapolis ICE nurse Alex Pretti's killing and memorial donations, then pivots to analyzing how lines like Auralee, Comoli, Evan Kinori, and Lemaire—once niche artisan projects—now dominate fashion discourse. They face two threats: supply-side carpetbaggers (Zara creating fake artisan stores, H&M pushing "H&M Atelier") and demand-side newcomers misunderstanding the appeal as IYKYK status signaling.

The essay's most provocative claim: good clothes have become seductive not as mere symbols but as "Things in Themselves." In 2026, "we have grown unprecedentedly tired and mistrustful of the World of Symbols and Signs. We want to engage, instead, with the World of Things in Themselves." The emphasis on natural dyes, visible construction, and "interior seams that no one else knows are there but you" offers an off-ramp from the anxiety of symbolic consumption—but only if practitioners resist collapsing into their own "earth-toned morass of marketing buzzwords."

[Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/cashless-is-swagless-wallets-beanies-hoodies-2026-footwear-trends-styling-swag) also weighed in on society's slide toward cashlessness, which "empowers and enriches no one as much as credit-card companies, payment-processors, and other Capitalism Superparasites." They warned of a near-future where "some enterprising 'disrupters' turn the ability to pay for things itself into a monthly paid-subscription service."

### 8. The Psychology of Suffering as Identity

**Coverage:** [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering)

[Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering) delivered Week 05's most philosophically ambitious piece: a 5,000+ word meditation on why suffering becomes seductive when it confirms identities we're not ready to abandon. Using Catherine from Wuthering Heights as her anchor, she argued that we don't undergo suffering passively—we inhabit it, protect it, and organize expectations around it until it acquires "the status of an interpretive instrument rather than an experience."

> "Suffering becomes seductive when it offers identity without risk. When it allows you to remain someone without becoming someone new. When it spares you the terror of desire returning." — [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering)

The essay's core thesis: pain explains us better than desire, which makes it dangerously comfortable. "I used to wake up already tired, the fatigue arriving in advance of the day itself, carried by the certainty of how it would unfold. Memory before tea. Longing before noon. Silence dressed up as depth." Healing threatens this structure because it's destabilizing—"Who are you without the wound?"

Tamara identified suffering's social functions: moral exemption ("People expect less of you"), aesthetic distinction (art rewards "the artist who bleeds onto the page"), and narrative coherence (explaining your hesitation, distance, and refusal to risk). The unfinished relationship becomes an "emotional reliquary" you revisit to preserve "a sort of ache that reassures you you are still alive."

> "Sometimes we don't want closure because closure would expose how much of the story was carried by us alone." — [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering)

Her most unsettling observation: "Uncertainty is far more destabilising than grief. Grief at least has contours. It gives the day a tone. You wake up knowing how you will feel. There is grim relief in that." The piece ends without resolution, only "the unremarkable decision to let a story end even if it once explained everything."

## Cross-Newsletter Patterns

### The Delegation Dilemma

Three newsletters explored different dimensions of effective delegation: [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower) to AI agents, [Scott Werner](https://worksonmymachine.ai/p/the-different-shapes-of-think-before) to oracles/LLMs, and [The Whirl](https://thewhirl.substack.com/p/angus-fletcher-narrative-intelligence-ooda-commanders-intent-red-teaming-truth-psyop) via Commander's Intent. All converged on the insight that clarity of intent and evaluation capacity matter more than clever techniques. Mollick: "management 101 skills." Werner: "make the Oracle say what good is, out loud, before you make it do good." Fletcher: "Commander's Intent gives the ending and protects agency."

### Liveness vs. Instrumentality

[Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness) on useless machines, [Ajeya Cotra](https://acotra.substack.com/p/the-ea-psyche) on EA's radical empathy, and [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera) on Epic Aerospace converge on a theme of intrinsic vs. instrumental value. The useless machine exists to assert control over itself, not solve human problems. EA's challenge is caring about beings without reducing them to instrumental value. Montero's spacecraft rescue mission serves no commercial purpose—it's pure assertion of will against entropy.

### The AGI Marketing Smokescreen

[Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting) and [Ethan Mollick](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower) present radically different AI narratives that both acknowledge real capabilities. Merchant: AGI rhetoric is "a marketing device" used to justify labor extraction and regulatory evasion, repeating patterns from 1800s automation. Mollick: AI delegation already works at scale when paired with management expertise. Both are true: AI does transform work through better delegation, and AGI claims do provide cover for extractive practices. The question is which force dominates.

### Authenticity Under Pressure

[Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring), [Olga](https://dataanalysis.substack.com/p/celebrating-300th-newsletter), [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering), and [Eliza Brooke](https://elizabrooke.substack.com/p/a-global-guide-to-special-stores) all grappled with how authenticity gets corrupted or performative. Slow Clothes brands face carpetbaggers and status-seekers. Newsletter creators face the "quantity over quality" treadmill and burnout. Suffering becomes "curated" and gains "social signature." Special stores risk becoming Instagram content farms. The pattern: what starts as genuine response to need becomes legible, gains currency, then attracts extractive or performative engagement that hollows out the original value.

### Institutional Decay and Capture

[The Whirl](https://thewhirl.substack.com/p/the-status-quo-machine-hidden-system-turns-operators-into-culture-problems) on corporate status quo machines, [Core Memory](https://www.corememory.com/p/exclusive-theres-a-spaceship-epic-aerospace-chimera) on agencies refusing commercial missions, and [Brian Merchant](https://bloodinthemachine.substack.com/p/on-the-smokescreen-of-agi-and-fighting) on AI companies avoiding accountability documented systems prioritizing self-preservation over mission. Whether through consultants validating hunches as "best practices," agencies gatekeeping deep space antennas, or AGI rhetoric justifying deregulation, the pattern repeats: institutions that lose connection to purpose become defensive, performative, and eventually predatory toward those who remember the original mission.

### The Tyranny of Legibility

Multiple newsletters highlighted how making things legible—to algorithms, markets, or status games—distorts their nature. [Tamara](https://museguided.substack.com/p/the-seduction-of-suffering): "The danger is when [suffering] becomes legible in a way that life no longer is." [Blackbird Spyplane](https://www.blackbirdspyplane.com/p/are-good-clothes-boring): slow clothes becoming fashionable creates "the same market obstacle any trend faces once it reaches critical mass: customer fatigue." [Olga](https://dataanalysis.substack.com/p/celebrating-300th-newsletter): "Your audience isn't you"—what's obvious in your bubble requires translation, but translation risks oversimplification. The more something succeeds by being understood, the more it must compromise the complexity that made it valuable.
